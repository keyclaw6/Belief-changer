## Mechanism (reward / attention / brain)

- Negative correlation between self-reported hours of pornography use per week and right striatum (caudate nucleus) gray matter volume; additionally, lower functional connectivity between the right caudate and the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) — Kühn & Gallinat (2014), *JAMA Psychiatry* — high-impact fMRI study, correlation design (causation not established, authors note findings may reflect precondition rather than consequence) — [MIXED] [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience of Compulsive Use, ref 22]
  - CONTESTED BY: Authors themselves emphasize: "could reflect change in neural plasticity as a consequence of intense stimulation of the reward system... though this structural alteration could represent a precondition rather than a direct consequence." [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, refs 22–23]

- Compulsive Sexual Behaviour (CSB) subjects showed significantly greater activation of dorsal anterior cingulate, ventral striatum, and amygdala in response to sexual cues compared to healthy controls — this specific corticostriatal limbic network is the same implicated in drug-cue reactivity — Voon et al. (2014), *PLoS ONE* — controlled fMRI study (19 CSB vs. 19 controls) — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, ref 24]

- CSB subjects showed pronounced dissociation between "wanting" (sexual desire, significantly elevated) and "liking" (subjective pleasure, similar to or lower than controls) in response to sexual cues — this wanting/liking dissociation is the neurobiological hallmark of incentive sensitization theory — Voon et al. (2014), *PLoS ONE* — controlled fMRI study — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, ref 24]

- The I-PACE (Interaction of Person-Affect-Cognition-Execution) model synthesizes neurobiological and affective variables: early addiction stages are mediated by fronto-striatal circuits involving ventral striatum and amygdala (reward-seeking and emotion regulation); later stages shift to dorsal striatum activation, indicating a transition from goal-directed to habitual/automatic behavior, compounded by diminished prefrontal inhibitory control — Brand et al. (2016, updated 2019), *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews* — foundational theoretical model, widely cited — [SUPPORTED as a theoretical framework; individual predictions are MIXED pending empirical replication] [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, refs 26–27]

- DeltaFosB release strengthens neural pathways in response to dopamine flooding from pornography, making cue-triggered activation progressively easier to elicit — the brain's self-correcting system down-regulates dopamine and opioid receptors under frequent flooding, reducing their availability for natural rewards — [PARAPHRASE of mechanism described in SRC-B §Nature, citing neuroplasticity literature broadly] — [CONTESTED — mechanistic description accurate to the literature but cited here from a non-peer-reviewed book source; see Kühn/Voon for peer-reviewed corroboration] [source: SRC-B §Nature, p.14–15; corroborated by SRC-A §Neuroscience]

- A functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) study found that internet pornography addiction is associated with changes in brain function — cited in SRC-A as additional neuroimaging evidence — PMC article (2025), PMC12040873 — preliminary fNIRS study — [CONTESTED — quality tier unclear from secondary citation; fNIRS less precise than fMRI] [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, ref 21]

- CSB individuals show enhanced novelty preference for sexual images AND faster dACC (dorsal anterior cingulate) habituation to repeated sexual content; dACC habituation mediates novelty-seeking, providing a neurobiological mechanism for why users require ever-newer content to maintain arousal; cue-conditioning is simultaneously generalized beyond sexual stimuli — Banca et al. (2016), PMC4683093, controlled fMRI, 22 CSB males vs. 40 controls — [SUPPORTED — for the dACC habituation mechanism; cross-sectional; male CSB sample only] [source: exa-escalation-novelty.md; exa-contested-claims.md]

- Individuals with high PIPU tendency show larger late positive potential (LPP) ERP responses (500–800ms) to novel vs. familiar sexual images — first electrophysiological evidence that PIPU brains allocate more attentional resources to novel sexual stimuli; replicates Banca et al. (2016) behavioral finding with an objective brain measure — EEG/ERP oddball paradigm, N=52 (27 high-PIPU vs. 25 low-PIPU), 2022, PMC9259837 — [SUPPORTED — objective neurophysiological measure; small sample; cross-sectional; cannot determine if enhanced novelty processing precedes or results from heavy use] [source: exa-escalation-novelty.md]

- Sexual sensation seeking (SSS) predicts PIPU via increased engagement in online sexual activities; this pathway is moderated by the third-person effect and is primarily applicable to males; the escalating novelty demands of SSS drive PIPU — I-PACE model, moderated mediation, Chinese university sample (2019), PMC6426391 — [SUPPORTED — theory-grounded model with gender-stratified analysis; cross-sectional; SSS measure may overlap with PIPU] [source: exa-escalation-novelty.md]

- Repeated sexual experience causes ΔFosB accumulation in the nucleus accumbens (NAc), medial prefrontal cortex, VTA, and caudate putamen; ΔFosB in the NAc is critical for reinforcing effects of sexual behavior and experience-induced facilitation of sexual motivation — Pitchers et al. (2010), PMC2970635, controlled rat study with viral-mediated gene transfer — high-quality animal model — [SUPPORTED — for the ΔFosB sexual reward mechanism; extrapolation to pornography specifically requires caution] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

- ΔFosB accumulates stably in NAc and dorsal striatum after repeated drug or compulsive-behavior exposure and persists long after the stimulus ceases; functions as a "sustained molecular switch" converting acute reward responses into stable addictive adaptations; downstream effects include altered GluR2 (AMPA receptor) expression and dynorphin suppression — Nestler et al. (2001), PubMed 11572966, foundational review with inducible transgenic mouse models — [SUPPORTED — for the ΔFosB molecular switch mechanism; not directly tested in pornography-specific human compulsive use] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

- Sexual experience induces ΔFosB in the NAc via D1-receptor-dependent dopamine signaling, increases dendritic spine density, and cross-sensitizes animals to amphetamine reward — demonstrating that natural sexual rewards and drugs of abuse share common neural plasticity mechanisms — Pitchers et al. (2013), PMC3865508, controlled study with D1R blockade and viral-vector ΔFosB manipulation — [SUPPORTED — for shared neuroplasticity between sexual behavior and drug addiction; animal model only] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

- The male sexual refractory period is governed by a cooperative neurotransmitter network, not a single molecule; serotonin plays an inhibitory role via 5-HT1B receptors; prolactin's involvement is equivocal; no single "off switch" identified — comprehensive review synthesizing animal and human data (2018), ScienceDirect — [SUPPORTED — for multi-system refractory period model] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

- Prolactin released during copulation is neither sufficient nor necessary to establish the post-ejaculatory refractory period — challenging the dominant textbook hypothesis; serotonin and oxytocin are implicated as more likely contributors to post-orgasmic quiescence — rigorous pharmacological manipulation in two mouse strains, Nature Communications (2021) — [SUPPORTED — challenges prolactin-as-cause narrative; human mechanism remains incompletely characterized] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

## Effects (attention, mood, motivation, relationships, etc.)

- Pornography consumption frequency in the past year was significantly associated with self-objectification and body comparison — [PARAPHRASE of study cited in SRC-C Extra: Studies, PMID33582530] — cross-sectional design — [CONTESTED — single study, cross-sectional, causation not established] [source: SRC-C §Extra: Studies, ref 2]

- Higher scores on Problematic Pornography Consumption (PPC) scales result in significantly higher probability of self-reported erectile dysfunction, controlling for covariates — large-scale cross-sectional international web survey — Associations Between Online Pornography Consumption and Sexual Dysfunction in Young Men (2021), *PMC* — multivariate cross-sectional — [MIXED — cross-sectional; self-report; higher PPC (not all use) predicts ED] [source: SRC-A §PIED Literature, ref 33]

- Clinical review of young men presenting with erectile dysfunction (ED) and delayed ejaculation linked to heavy internet pornography use proposes a dual mechanism: (1) hyper-reactivity to internet pornography cues via glutamate inputs, and (2) downregulation of the reward system's response to normal rewards — Park et al. (2016), *Behavioral Sciences* — clinical review/case series — [CONTESTED — methodological and conflict-of-interest complaints were filed; journal corrected minor disclosures but refused retraction; causal claims remain controversial] [source: SRC-A §PIED Literature, refs 28–31]
  - CONTESTED BY: A 2023 systematic review (Bacopoulou et al., *PubMed*) encompassing literature 2013–2023 concluded "mere watching of pornography is not a significant risk factor for developing a sexual dysfunction"; the association is indirect, driven by problematic use, body dissatisfaction, and performance anxiety rather than exposure per se. [source: SRC-A §PIED Literature, ref 32]

- Emerging scientific consensus: dysregulated, compulsive pornography use (not moderate viewing) can trigger severe psychogenic erectile dysfunction via classical conditioning, wherein arousal becomes strictly tethered to digital hyperstimulation — this renders real-life encounters insufficient — [PARAPHRASE of convergent interpretation across refs 34–35] — [MIXED — supported directionally by multiple reviews but causation not established experimentally] [source: SRC-A §PIED Literature, refs 34–35]

- Present Hedonistic Time Perspective was moderately and positively related to hypersexuality and pornography use in two independent samples — PMID36081467 — cross-sectional — [CONTESTED — single cluster of studies; cross-sectional] [source: SRC-C §Extra: Studies, ref 4]

- Women's reports of their male partners' pornography use are associated with lower relationship satisfaction and higher psychological distress; relationship trust fully mediates these associations; the effect persists regardless of women's own attitudes toward pornography — N=359 college women, mediation analysis, Bridges & Morokoff (2015), Archives of Sexual Behavior — [SUPPORTED — for trust as the key clinical mechanism; cross-sectional; heterosexual women only] [source: exa-relationship-impacts.md]

- Pornography use frequency is associated with lower relational satisfaction — a small but significant and replicable effect consistent with meta-analytic findings; effect is present for men (especially White men) but not women in this sample — probability sample of 750+ graduate students in committed relationships (2022) — [MIXED — small effect; gender and race moderate strength; graduate student sample; causal direction unclear] [source: exa-relationship-impacts.md]

- Video pornography use is associated with lower relationship and sexual satisfaction for both the user and their partner; picture use is associated with lower relationship satisfaction; women's audio use uniquely associates with higher own sexual satisfaction — N=706 cohabiting couples, actor-partner interdependence modeling (2024) — [SUPPORTED — for video as most relationally harmful medium; cross-sectional; does not distinguish recreational from problematic use] [source: exa-relationship-impacts.md]

- Pornography use frequency and duration alone are NOT associated with erectile dysfunction or premature ejaculation; self-perceived IP addiction IS associated with — and uniquely predicts — increased ED and sexual dissatisfaction — N=942 heterosexual men 18–44, cross-sectional, ScienceDirect (2022) — [MIXED — distinguishes objective use from subjective perceived addiction; Reddit sample] [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]
  - CONTESTED BY: No consistent association between mere pornography use and erectile dysfunction found across three samples including a 4-wave longitudinal (Grubbs & Gola, 2019, PubMed 30621919); problematic use and ED co-occur but do not predict each other's trajectories over time. [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]

- SEM exposure in early adolescence (age ~13, Taiwan Youth Project, 13-year prospective follow-up) predicts: 31.7% more likely to have early sexual debut, 27.4% more likely to engage in unsafe sex, and on average 3+ sexual partners by age 24; multi-modal SEM exposure amplifies all effects — IV estimation controlling for pre-existing propensity — [MIXED — addresses SEM broadly, not internet pornography specifically; non-Western sample] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]

- High-frequency cyber-pornography exposure (several times daily) in adults who first accessed pornography before age 18 is robustly associated with PPU scores above the clinical threshold; early onset + high frequency is the highest-risk profile, particularly for men — N=200 UK adults, regression with validated PPU measures (2026) — [SUPPORTED — for early onset + high frequency as highest-risk profile; cross-sectional; modest N; retrospective] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]

- After orgasm, dopamine drops below baseline (temporary "crash"), while prolactin surges (satiety), androgens decrease, and serotonin rises; this coordinated neurochemical cascade produces both the refractory period and the "post-nut doom" described by users — synthesis citing Holstege et al. 2003, Kruger et al. 2003/2012; not primary research — [MIXED — synthesizes peer-reviewed sources; some specific citations contested or imprecise] [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]

- Orgasm produces simultaneous release of dopamine, oxytocin, endorphins, and vasopressin with temporary deactivation of the lateral orbitofrontal cortex (self-monitoring/behavioral control); post-orgasm, prolactin surges in men producing drowsiness/withdrawal; a 15–45 min post-orgasmic consolidation window requires sustained skin-to-skin contact for secondary oxytocin release — clinical neuroscience synthesis citing Georgiadis & Kringelbach (2012) neuroimaging; couples therapy application — [MIXED — not a primary research article; clinically applied synthesis] [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]

- Higher testosterone concentrations were observed following a period of abstinence from masturbation; effect potentially mediated by dopaminergic systems — PMID11760788 — small experimental study — [CONTESTED — single small study; effect size and clinical significance unclear] [source: SRC-C §Extra: Studies, ref 5]

## Diagnosis / frameworks / prevalence

- In 2018 (adopted 2019, effective 2022), the WHO included Compulsive Sexual Behaviour Disorder (CSBD) in ICD-11 as an *impulse control disorder* — deliberately not classified as a behavioral addiction — diagnostic criteria require: persistent pattern of failure to control intense, repetitive sexual impulses; behavior becomes central focus of life; neglect of health and responsibilities; continuation despite adverse consequences or deriving little satisfaction — WHO ICD-11 (2019) — authoritative international classification — [SUPPORTED as a formal diagnostic category; the impulse-control rather than addiction classification is the WHO's deliberate position] [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 36–38]

- ICD-11 CSBD guidelines explicitly state that "distress that is entirely related to moral judgments and disapproval about sexual impulses, urges, or behaviours is not sufficient" to meet the diagnostic requirement — this exclusion criterion directly addresses the moral incongruence confound — WHO ICD-11 (2019) — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 37–38]

- The Moral Incongruence Model (Grubbs): self-perceived "addiction" to pornography is frequently driven not by objective neurobiological dysregulation but by psychological conflict between behavior and deeply held moral/religious beliefs — large-scale cross-sectional and longitudinal data demonstrate "moral incongruence predicted perceived addiction above and beyond IPU itself" — Grubbs et al. (multiple, 2018–ongoing), multiple journals — replicated cross-sectional and longitudinal studies — [SUPPORTED as a real and measurable phenomenon; does not rule out concurrent genuine PPU] [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 39–42]

- Recent methodological work shows that while moral incongruence is a powerful predictor of distress, it does not mutually exclude objective pathology — many respondents reporting severe moral incongruence concurrently meet rigorous clinical thresholds for genuine PPU — suggests overlapping, not mutually exclusive, etiologies — [PARAPHRASE of convergent finding] — multiple authors — [MIXED — complex overlap; not resolvable by current cross-sectional designs] [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 44–45]

- Three distinct at-risk profiles exist in male pornography users (US N=1,356; UK N=944; latent profile analysis): "at risk for religiosity-based MI only" (4–8%), "at risk for PPU only" (6–10%), and "at risk for co-occurring PPU and MI" (6–8%); religiosity-based MI group alone does NOT show heightened psychological distress or treatment-seeking — Grubbs et al. (2025), PubMed 40126576, two-country LPA — [MIXED — large samples, rigorous LPA, 2025 data; cross-sectional; male only; online samples] [source: exa-contested-claims.md]

- After 10 years of research, moral incongruence is observable across cultures and languages, uniquely tied to religious beliefs, and may apply to behaviors beyond pornography; recent cross-cultural validations (Bőthe et al. 2025) and clinical sample studies confirm generalizability — Grubbs & Floyd (2025), Springer, 10-year narrative review of 63+ studies — [SUPPORTED — for the moral incongruence pathway specifically; narrative review, no original data] [source: exa-contested-claims.md]

- CSB shows high transience in non-clinical samples — individuals with higher baseline CSB scores experience more pronounced reduction in severity over 1 year; anxiety/depression and sexual sensation seeking are the most consistent predictors of continued CSB trajectory; baseline severity is the strongest overall predictor — prospective longitudinal design (2023) — [MIXED — non-clinical sample only; does not distinguish PPU specifically from broader CSB] [source: exa-contested-claims.md]

- Adolescents who did NOT use pornography during adolescence but watched mainstream media with moderate-to-severe sexual content were more likely to use pornography in adulthood; for those who already used pornography during adolescence, prior use (not mainstream media) was the dominant predictor of adult use — longitudinal study using IMDb parental guide ratings as proxy measure (2026) — [MIXED — novel pathway identified; relies on retrospective recall; IMDb rating is imperfect proxy] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]

- The Prause et al. (2013) EEG study (N=52 self-identified problem viewers) found larger P300 amplitude differences to pleasant sexual stimuli were negatively related to sexual desire and not related to hypersexuality measures — Prause et al. (2013), *PubMed* PMID24693355 — EEG study, 52 participants — [CONTESTED — Prause argues this challenges the addiction model; however, the study's design and interpretation are themselves disputed; the finding runs counter to Voon et al.'s fMRI cue-reactivity data] [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 46–47]
  - CONTESTED BY: Voon et al. (2014) fMRI data showing heightened cue-reactivity in CSB subjects [source: SRC-A §Neuroscience, ref 24]. Methodological differences (EEG P300 vs. fMRI BOLD) and sample selection differences (self-identified vs. clinically assessed) limit direct comparison.

- Mean differences suggest gay/bisexual men and women tend to report more pornography use than heterosexual men and women — PMID35753890 — cross-sectional — [CONTESTED — single study; prevalence data useful for context but not effect evidence] [source: SRC-C §Extra: Studies, ref 3]

- 48.8% of adolescents aged 12–17 (N=664, Spain, 2025) had consumed pornography at least once; pornography users scored significantly higher on traditional sexist attitudes (Cohen's d=0.29) and had 2–6x higher rates of risky online/offline sexual behaviors (e.g., sexting 6x higher) — cross-sectional, Frontiers in Public Health — [MIXED — cross-sectional; cannot determine direction of causality between sexist attitudes and pornography use] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]

- Earlier pornography exposure, higher viewing frequency, positive affective responses at initial exposure, sexual impulsivity, and IP-related sexual beliefs all independently predict self-assessed problematic pornography use in emerging adults; majority of males recall first exposure at ages 12–14; 28.2% of males and 23.7% of females report first exposure at ages 9–11 — UK university emerging adult sample (2023) — [SUPPORTED — for early exposure as independent risk factor; retrospective recall subject to memory bias] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]

## Intervention efficacy (what actually helps)

- CBT meta-analyses of RCTs demonstrate large within-subject effect sizes for CBT interventions targeting PPU: improvements in quality of life, reductions in depressive symptoms, and significant decreases in time spent watching pornography, stable at follow-up — systematic review protocol by multiple authors (PMC8340575) + comprehensive meta-analysis (PMC12231474) — meta-analyses of RCTs — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Intervention Efficacy, refs 52–54]

- ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) landmark RCT by Crosby & Twohig (2016): 93% reduction in viewing in the ACT group vs. waitlist; 54% reported complete cessation at post-treatment; gains maintained at 3-month follow-up — Crosby & Twohig (2016), *PubMed* PMID27157029 — randomized trial — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Intervention Efficacy, refs 56–57]

- Mindfulness-based approaches, when integrated with CBT/ACT protocols, improve non-judgmental present-moment awareness, interrupt automaticity of habitual cue-response patterns, and reduce impulsive reactions to triggers — multiple authors — systematic review evidence (Semantic Scholar, preregistered review) — [SUPPORTED as adjunct; standalone RCT evidence thinner] [source: SRC-A §Intervention Efficacy, refs 50–51, 58]

- Psychotherapy for PPU demonstrates large effect sizes overall in a comprehensive meta-analysis (Journal of Behavioral Addictions, 2025): reductions in PPU frequency, craving, and related psychological distress — multiple authors — comprehensive meta-analysis — [SUPPORTED] [source: SRC-A §Intervention Efficacy, ref 55]

- Mayo Clinic treatment guidelines identify CBT as first-line, with ACT and mindfulness as effective adjuncts — Mayo Clinic clinical guidance — clinical guideline level — [SUPPORTED as clinical consensus] [source: SRC-A §Intervention Efficacy, ref 50]

## CONTESTED — do NOT overstate (would damage credibility)

- CONTESTED CLAIM — "Pornography causes permanent brain damage": Forum communities and some advocacy sources claim pornography permanently "fries" dopamine receptors or causes irreversible brain damage. The peer-reviewed interpretation of Kühn & Gallinat (2014) frames observed structural differences as evidence of *neuroplasticity* — the brain's reversible adaptation to overstimulation — not permanent traumatic injury. Abstinence and behavioral change allow recovery. — [SUPPORTED: neuroplastic framing is reversible] — [source: SRC-A §Contested Claims, ref 22]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — "PIED is an inevitable biological consequence of any porn use": Recovery communities treat PIED as universal and biological. The rigorous literature (Bacopoulou et al., 2023 systematic review) concludes that moderate non-compulsive viewing does not significantly cause physiological ED; PIED is a complex psychogenic phenomenon tied to dysregulated use, performance anxiety, and classical conditioning. — [MIXED — PIED real for problematic users; not a universal consequence of exposure] — [source: SRC-A §PIED Literature / Contested Claims, refs 32, 34]
  - ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE: Grubbs & Gola (2019), three samples including longitudinal, finds no causal trajectory relationship between problematic use and ED over time — co-occurrence likely reflects shared underlying factors (anxiety, distress) rather than pornography causing ED physiologically. [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]
  - ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE: N=942 cross-sectional study (2022) confirms: frequency and duration of use are NOT associated with ED; only self-perceived addiction predicts ED. [source: exa-pied-and-post-nut.md]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — Universal validity of the "addict" label: The NoFap community universally uses the addict identity as a recovery tool. Academia is fractured: Grubbs' Moral Incongruence research demonstrates many self-described "addicts" do not exhibit neurobiological dysregulation but rather suffer distress from religious/moral guilt creating the *perception* of addiction. The WHO's deliberate classification of CSBD as impulse control disorder (not addiction) reflects this uncertainty — they cited absence of physiological tolerance and withdrawal characteristic of severe substance abuse. — [CONTESTED — label is useful to some, harmful to others; distress is real regardless of label] — [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks / Contested Claims, refs 38–41]
  - CONTESTED BY: Moral incongruence and genuine PPU are not mutually exclusive — many meeting high moral-incongruence criteria also meet clinical PPU thresholds. The label debate does not erase functional impairment. [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 44–45]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — Addiction model vs. high-sexual-desire model: Prause, Ley, and skeptics argue high-frequency pornography use is better explained as non-pathological high sexual desire, not addiction; they critique the addiction model as commercially motivated and poorly designed. The Prause (2013) EEG finding of inverse P300/desire correlation is the primary empirical challenge to the addiction framework. — [CONTESTED — genuine academic dispute; not resolved] — [source: SRC-A §Diagnostic Frameworks, refs 46–49]
  - CONTESTED BY: Voon et al. (2014) fMRI showing addiction-parallel cue-reactivity in CSB subjects [ref 24]; ICD-11 CSBD classification providing clinical recognition of the disorder regardless of addiction label [refs 36–38]. The debate is ongoing with no clear resolution in the literature reviewed.

- CONTESTED CLAIM — Dopamine claims from self-help books (SRC-B, SRC-C): Both EasyPeasy and Burgeon make specific neurochemical claims (DeltaFosB pathway, dopamine receptor downregulation, acetylcholine depletion from pornography, extensive nutrient loss from ejaculation). These mechanistic descriptions draw on real neuroscience concepts but are presented in non-peer-reviewed books without citation precision. The broad direction (dopaminergic desensitization as a mechanism) is supported by Kühn/Voon/Brand; specific claims about acetylcholine, nutrient depletion from semen, and "permanent receptor damage" are NOT supported by peer-reviewed evidence cited in these sources. — [CONTESTED — broad mechanism credible; specific claims uncorroborated or unsupported] — [source: SRC-B §Nature; SRC-C §Effects]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — NoFap community "superpowers" narrative: Community discourse frequently attributes dramatic physical and social benefits (heightened attractiveness, supernatural confidence, testosterone spikes) to abstinence. These benefits are not substantiated by controlled research; what users describe is plausibly the removal of compulsive-use harms rather than genuine abstinence-specific gains. The one testosterone study cited (PMID11760788) involves 7 days abstinence and shows a transient spike; clinical significance is unclear. — [CONTESTED — removal-of-negatives framing more defensible than direct benefit claims; see Burgeon's own framing: "the benefits of leaving porn/masturbation are not direct benefits but are just the end of negatives"] — [source: SRC-A §Contested Claims; SRC-C §Effects, p.15]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — "Escalation to violent content is universal and automatic": Recovery communities and some advocacy sources treat escalation to increasingly extreme/violent material as an inevitable consequence of regular use. The first longitudinal test of the Content Progression Thesis (CPT) — PROBIOPS study, 248 male adolescents, 5-wave 24-month design — found preference for violent/coercive pornography DECREASED over 24 months and was unrelated to pornography use trajectories; the CPT is not supported for adolescents over 2 years. — [CONTESTED — challenges CPT for adolescents specifically; does not rule out escalation in adult compulsive users or to non-violent extreme content] [source: exa-escalation-novelty.md; exa-adolescent-effects.md]
  - CONTESTED BY: EEG study (N=52, 2022) finds individuals with high PIPU tendency show larger LPP responses to novel vs. familiar sexual images — first electrophysiological evidence of enhanced novelty processing driving continued consumption. Banca et al. (2016) fMRI finds CSB associated with faster dACC habituation to repeated content, driving stronger novelty preference — a neurobiological mechanism for escalation. [source: exa-escalation-novelty.md]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — "Prolactin causes the post-ejaculatory refractory period": Popular science and self-help sources frequently attribute the post-orgasm refractory period to prolactin surge. Rigorous pharmacological research in two mouse strains (Nature Communications, 2021) demonstrates prolactin is neither sufficient nor necessary to establish the refractory period; serotonin and oxytocin are more likely contributors. — [SUPPORTED for the challenge to prolactin-as-cause; human mechanism incompletely characterized] [source: exa-neuroscience.md]

- CONTESTED CLAIM — "No longitudinal association between adolescent pornography use and well-being": Some longitudinal studies (Croatian adolescent panels, N=455 and N=858, 6-wave and 12-month designs) find no significant association between pornography use growth and changes in self-esteem or depression/anxiety for male adolescents. However, for female adolescents in one panel, higher baseline use predicted lower well-being 12 months later, and lower well-being predicted increased use — suggesting a bidirectional loop specifically for girls. — [MIXED — gender-specific, sample-specific; does not address early or heavy use] [source: exa-adolescent-effects.md]
