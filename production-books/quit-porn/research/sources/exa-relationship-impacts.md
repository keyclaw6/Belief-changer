## Sub-question 5: Partner/relationship impacts — peer-reviewed studies on satisfaction, betrayal, couples data

---

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11199-015-0518-5
  DATE: 2015
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 359 young adult college women (US); online survey; partners' perceived pornography use
  KEY_CLAIM: Women's reports of their male partners' pornography use are associated with lower relationship satisfaction and higher psychological distress; relationship trust fully mediates these associations; the effect persists regardless of women's own attitudes toward pornography.
  MECHANISM: Perceived pornography use erodes relationship trust; when trust is low (especially for women with low-to-moderate relationship investment), psychological distress and dissatisfaction follow. Women's own attitudes toward pornography do not buffer the negative effect of perceiving their partner uses it.
  STRENGTH: N=359; mediation and moderation analyses; trust identified as the key mechanism for clinical intervention.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Cross-sectional; perceiving vs actual partner use conflated; heterosexual women only; does not address frequency or content of partner's use.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.661347/pdf
  DATE: 2021
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Four samples of heterosexual couples; two cross-sectional (N=200, N=207) and two longitudinal (N=77, N=277); Canada
  KEY_CLAIM: Partners who watch pornography TOGETHER report higher relationship and sexual satisfaction (effect not moderated by gender); solitary pornography use is associated with lower satisfaction only when the partner uses little or no pornography — it is partner dissimilarity in porn use (not use itself) that predicts relational harm.
  MECHANISM: Dissimilarity effect: when one partner uses pornography alone and the other does not, conflict arises from different sexual expectations, perceived infidelity, and relationship inequality. Sex-drive dissimilarity (not porn attitudes per se) statistically accounts for much of the dissimilarity-satisfaction link.
  STRENGTH: Multiple independent samples including longitudinal designs; couples-level (dyadic) analysis rather than individual-level.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Heterosexual couples only; does not capture compulsive/problematic use specifically; "shared use" may not address secret solitary escalation.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38858232/
  DATE: 2024
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 706 adult cohabiting couples (1,368 participants; mean age 32); online self-report
  KEY_CLAIM: Video pornography use is associated with lower relationship AND sexual satisfaction for both the user and their partner; picture use is associated with lower relationship satisfaction and lower partner sexual satisfaction; written material use is associated with lower individual sexual satisfaction; women's audio pornography use uniquely associates with higher own sexual satisfaction while men's audio use associates with lower partner satisfaction.
  MECHANISM: Actor–partner interdependence model captures spillover effects from one partner's use to the other's satisfaction; medium-specific content and arousal patterns produce different relational effects.
  STRENGTH: Large couple sample; actor-partner interdependence modeling; distinguishes media types.
  STANCE: supported (for video as most harmful medium)
  GAP: Cross-sectional; self-report; does not distinguish recreational from problematic use.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36042069/
  DATE: 2022
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 750+ graduate students in committed relationships; campus-representative probability sample
  KEY_CLAIM: More frequent pornography use is associated with lower relational satisfaction — a small but significant and replicable effect consistent with meta-analytic findings; this effect is present for men (especially White men) but not women in this sample.
  MECHANISM: The association may reflect broader sexual dissatisfaction or fantasy-reality discrepancy for male users; gender and race moderate the strength of the association.
  STRENGTH: Probability sample (not convenience); replicates meta-analytic findings in a new context; moderation analyses for gender, race, orientation, religiosity.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Graduate student sample (well-educated, may differ from general population); effect is small; causal direction unclear.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10591-026-09785-z
  DATE: 2026-06-03
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 31 couples who stayed together after infidelity; qualitative deductive analysis; average 28 years together; infidelity types included pornography-related infidelity (25.8% of sample)
  KEY_CLAIM: Six core resilience components support healing after pornography-related and other infidelity: open communication, individual healing, meaning-making, reinvented normalcy, therapy (couples + individual), and the ongoing nature of healing. Therapy — specifically couples therapy, used by 96% of the healing participants — was a central enabler identified by all participants.
  MECHANISM: Betrayal trauma activates stress systems (the dump's existing data on nervous/immune system effects is corroborated here); healing requires both individual and dyadic processes; transparency, rather than just communication, is a specific sub-component identified as central to recovery from sexual/pornography betrayal.
  STRENGTH: Qualitative analysis of 31 couples with varied infidelity types; clinically grounded; 2026 data; includes pornography use as explicit infidelity category.
  VERBATIM: "Therapy helped the SPs work through guilt, shame, and compulsive behaviors, and the NSP process insecurity, grief, and anger"
  STANCE: supported (for therapy as central to recovery)
  GAP: Small qualitative sample; mostly White, married, Christian couples; selection bias (only couples who stayed together).
