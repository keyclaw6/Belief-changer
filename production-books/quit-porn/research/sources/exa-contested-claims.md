## Sub-question 2: Contested-claims landscape — moral incongruence model vs addiction model, latest data

---

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10508-018-1248-x
  DATE: 2019
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Systematic review + meta-analysis; multiple samples; integrative model paper (Grubbs, Perry et al.)
  KEY_CLAIM: Pornography-related problems — including feelings of addiction — are in many cases better explained as functions of moral incongruence (discrepancy between pornography-related beliefs and behaviors) rather than objective neurobiological dysregulation; religiosity/moral disapproval predicts perceived addiction above and beyond actual use.
  MECHANISM: When individuals believe pornography is wrong but continue using it, the resulting psychological conflict registers as "feeling addicted." The key instrument (CPUI-9) measures perceived problems, not objective compulsivity — most PPU measures are essentially self-reports of moral-cognitive conflict, not physiological dependency.
  STRENGTH: Systematic review and meta-analysis across multiple samples; foundational model paper now cited >1000 times.
  STANCE: contested
  GAP: Does not deny that some individuals have genuine neurobiological dysregulation; cannot disentangle the overlapping population.

---

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40429-025-00675-2
  DATE: 2025-06-23
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Narrative/developmental review of moral incongruence research (10-year review; Grubbs & Floyd 2025)
  KEY_CLAIM: After 10 years of research, moral incongruence is observable across cultures and languages, is uniquely tied to religious beliefs, replicates in numerous countries, and may apply to behaviors beyond pornography; the model has been revised and critiqued but remains influential.
  MECHANISM: The review synthesizes evidence that moral incongruence accounts for a distinct pathway to perceived PPU that does not depend on actual compulsivity. Recent cross-cultural validations (Bőthe et al. 2025, global investigation) and clinical sample studies confirm the model's generalizability.
  STRENGTH: Narrative review of 10-year research program; cites 63+ studies.
  STANCE: supported (for the moral incongruence pathway specifically)
  GAP: Review article — does not provide original data. Does not resolve the question of what proportion of self-identified "addicts" have genuine PPU vs moral-incongruence-driven perceived addiction.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40126576/
  DATE: 2025
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Two online samples of male porn users, US (N=1,356) and UK (N=944); latent profile analysis
  KEY_CLAIM: Three distinct at-risk profiles exist: "at risk for religiosity-based MI only" (4–8%), "at risk for PPU only" (6–10%), and "at risk for co-occurring PPU and MI" (6–8%); critically, the religiosity-based MI group alone does NOT show heightened psychological distress or treatment-seeking tendencies — challenging the assumption that moral incongruence always drives clinical need.
  MECHANISM: Latent profile analysis distinguishes genuine PPU (which co-occurs with psychological distress and treatment-seeking) from religiosity-based MI (which does not, when PPU is controlled). The "at risk for co-occurring PPU and MI" group (6–8%) is the most clinically complex, suggesting some individuals have both real compulsivity AND moral guilt compounding the experience.
  STRENGTH: Large two-country samples; rigorous LPA methodology; 2025 data.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Cross-sectional; male only; online samples. Does not capture clinical treatment populations who may have more severe presentations.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11469-023-01061-7
  DATE: 2023-05-05
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Non-clinical longitudinal sample; 1-year follow-up of CSB symptoms
  KEY_CLAIM: CSB shows high transience in non-clinical samples — individuals with higher baseline CSB scores experience a more pronounced reduction in severity over time; baseline severity is the strongest predictor of trajectory at 1 year; traditional risk factors (gender, religiousness, self-esteem) barely predict the natural course of CSB once baseline is controlled.
  MECHANISM: CSB may be self-limiting in sub-clinical populations; anxiety/depression and sexual sensation seeking are the most consistent predictors of continued CSB trajectory, aligning with negative-reinforcement coping models.
  STRENGTH: Prospective longitudinal design; addresses limitations of retrospective clinical samples; n not specified in available extract.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Non-clinical sample only — may underrepresent severe/persistent PPU. Does not distinguish PPU specifically from broader CSB.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4683093/
  DATE: 2016
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 22 CSB males vs 40 age-matched controls; behavioral + fMRI tasks (novelty and cue-conditioning, Cambridge University — Voon lab follow-up)
  KEY_CLAIM: CSB individuals have enhanced preference for sexual novelty AND generalized enhancement of conditioning to both sexual and monetary rewards; greater dorsal anterior cingulate (dACC) habituation to repeated sexual images correlates with enhanced novelty-seeking — providing a neurobiological mechanism for escalation to novel content.
  MECHANISM: dACC habituation drives novelty preference; this is dissociable from cue-conditioning (which relates to attentional bias). Both mechanisms operate simultaneously in CSB, suggesting two distinct pathways to compulsive novelty-seeking.
  STRENGTH: Controlled fMRI study with behavioral tasks; direct extension of the Voon et al. 2014 cue-reactivity findings.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Small sample (22 CSB); all male; cross-sectional; cannot determine if dACC habituation is cause or consequence of CSB.
