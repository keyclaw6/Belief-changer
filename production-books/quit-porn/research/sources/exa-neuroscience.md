## Sub-question 1: Dopamine/neuroscience mechanisms — DeltaFosB, desensitization, prefrontal cortex downregulation

---

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2970635/
  DATE: 2010
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Male rats; sexual experience study, NAc/limbic brain regions
  KEY_CLAIM: Repeated sexual experience causes ΔFosB accumulation in the nucleus accumbens (NAc), medial prefrontal cortex, VTA and caudate putamen; ΔFosB in the NAc is critical for the reinforcing effects of sexual behavior and experience-induced facilitation of sexual motivation.
  MECHANISM: DA release from VTA during sexual behavior induces ΔFosB in NAc via D1 receptor activation; ΔFosB represses c-Fos (a marker of acute activation), indicating a shift from acute reward signaling to stable habituation. ΔFosB acts as a transcriptional activator/repressor of downstream genes (cdk5, dynorphin, GluR2), producing long-term neuroplastic changes.
  STRENGTH: Controlled animal study with viral-mediated gene transfer; causal manipulation of ΔFosB confirmed behavioral effects.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Extrapolation from rats to humans requires caution; does not test pornography specifically, only sexual reward.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11572966/
  DATE: 2001
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Nucleus accumbens/dorsal striatum; drug addiction/natural reward model (Nestler lab)
  KEY_CLAIM: ΔFosB accumulates stably in NAc and dorsal striatum after repeated drug or compulsive-behavior exposure and persists long after the stimulus ceases; it functions as a "sustained molecular switch" converting acute drug responses into stable addictive adaptations.
  MECHANISM: Unlike other Fos-family proteins (which are rapidly degraded), ΔFosB isoforms are extraordinarily stable. With each repeated exposure a small amount accumulates; over time levels build to cause lasting gene-expression changes. Downstream effects include altered GluR2 (AMPA receptor) expression — reducing overall AMPA current and rendering NAc neurons more sensitive to inhibitory/reinforcing effects — and dynorphin suppression, which disinhibits VTA dopamine neurons and enhances reward.
  STRENGTH: Foundational review; inducible transgenic mouse models confirm behavioral sensitization via ΔFosB.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Based on drug-reward models; whether identical mechanisms operate for pornography-specific compulsive use has not been directly tested in humans.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3865508/
  DATE: 2013
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Male rats; sexual experience and drug cross-sensitization study
  KEY_CLAIM: Sexual experience induces ΔFosB in the NAc via D1-receptor-dependent dopamine signaling, increasing dendritic spine density and cross-sensitizing animals to amphetamine reward — demonstrating that natural sexual rewards and drugs of abuse share common neural plasticity mechanisms.
  MECHANISM: D1R activation in NAc during sexual behavior is necessary for both ΔFosB induction and spinogenesis; the abstinence period after sexual experience is required for sensitized drug reward, suggesting ΔFosB mediates plasticity changes during the withdrawal/abstinence window. ΔFosB activates cdk5 and NF-κB (increasing spine density) and represses c-Fos.
  STRENGTH: Controlled study with pharmacological D1R blockade and viral-vector ΔFosB manipulation confirming causal role.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Animal model; does not measure pornography use or human compulsive sexual behavior directly.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-020-01570-4
  DATE: 2021
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Male mice (two strains); post-ejaculatory refractory period (PERP) biology
  KEY_CLAIM: Prolactin released during copulation is neither sufficient nor necessary to establish the post-ejaculatory refractory period — challenging the dominant textbook hypothesis; serotonin and oxytocin (which stimulate prolactin release) are implicated as more likely contributors to post-orgasmic sexual quiescence.
  MECHANISM: Pharmacological elevation of prolactin (domperidone) before mating did not induce a refractory state; pharmacological suppression (bromocriptine) during mating did not shorten the refractory period. Prolactin release may be a "side effect" of dopamine drop and serotonin/oxytocin rise at orgasm, rather than the causal driver.
  STRENGTH: Rigorous pharmacological manipulation in two strains; challenges widespread popular-science narrative.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Mouse model; human PERP mechanism remains incompletely characterized. Does not address post-nut clarity's psychological components.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0149763417304888
  DATE: 2018
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Review of male sexual refractory period neurobiology across species
  KEY_CLAIM: The male sexual refractory period is governed by a cooperative network of neurotransmitters and brain regions — not any single molecule; serotonin plays an inhibitory role in sexual behavior via 5-HT1B receptors; prolactin's involvement is equivocal.
  MECHANISM: Multiple interacting systems: serotonin inhibits sexual activity (evidence from SSRI sexual side effects and 5-HT1B agonist studies); dopamine drop post-orgasm; decreased androgen receptor density in medial preoptic area. No single "off switch."
  STRENGTH: Comprehensive review synthesizing animal and human data.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Mechanism remains incompletely resolved; most data from animal models.

- SOURCE_TYPE: news
  URL: https://shawketalkhayal.com/postcoital-neurochemistry-the-blues-and-the-highs/
  DATE: 2024-01-14
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: General scientific blog; postcoital neurochemistry synthesis
  KEY_CLAIM: After orgasm, dopamine drops below baseline (creating a temporary "crash" similar to drug withdrawal), while prolactin surges (providing satiety), androgens decrease (reducing sexual drive), and serotonin rises (producing calm) — this coordinated neurochemical cascade is what produces both the refractory period and the "post-nut doom" described by users.
  MECHANISM: Orgasm activates reward pathways identically to a heroin rush (Holstege et al., 2003); post-orgasm dopamine sub-baseline drop is associated with depression, low energy, social anxiety. Prolactin surge (Kruger et al., 2003, 2012) is a reliable orgasm biomarker and dopamine inhibitor. Androgen receptor downregulation in medial preoptic area further reduces sexual drive.
  STRENGTH: Synthesis citing peer-reviewed sources; not primary research.
  VERBATIM: "after the rush of orgasm, dopamine levels drop below baseline, similar to what happens during withdrawal from drugs of abuse. Low dopamine levels are associated with depression, low energy, lack of ambition, social anxiety"
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Blog synthesis, not primary research; some citations (Holstege 2003 orgasm = heroin rush) are commonly cited but not from a single clean study.
