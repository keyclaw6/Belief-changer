## Sub-question 4: Adolescent/developmental effects — age of first exposure, sexual scripts, long-term outcomes

---

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7147756/
  DATE: 2020
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Taiwan Youth Project; 7th grade cohort (mean age 13.3) followed to age 24; two-stage least squares regression with pubertal timing as instrumental variable
  KEY_CLAIM: Sexually explicit media (SEM) exposure in early adolescence (age ~13) predicts risky sexual behaviors in emerging adulthood: 31.7% more likely to have early sexual debut (before 17), 27.4% more likely to engage in unsafe sex, and on average 3+ sexual partners by age 24; multi-modal SEM exposure amplifies all effects.
  MECHANISM: SEM delivers permissive sexual scripts depicting gratification without negative consequences; these scripts are internalized during a developmentally sensitive period and enacted in offline behavior. Causal-like inference supported by IV estimation controlling for pre-existing propensity.
  STRENGTH: Prospective longitudinal design (13 years); IV estimation addresses selection bias; large sample; cross-cultural (non-Western) context.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Addresses "sexually explicit media" broadly, not internet pornography specifically; Taiwanese sample limits direct generalizability to Western adolescents.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2026.1815849/full
  DATE: 2026-04-21
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Adults (N=200) who first accessed cyber-pornography before age 18; UK-based sample
  KEY_CLAIM: High-frequency cyber-pornography exposure (FC-PE) is robustly associated with higher PPU scores in adults who started viewing before 18; those viewing several times daily score above the PPU threshold (mean >76), while lower-frequency viewers score below; early onset + high frequency is the highest-risk profile, particularly for men.
  MECHANISM: Repeated rewarding stimuli produce dopamine surges in the mesolimbic pathway, reinforcing the behavior. Over time, neuroadaptation reduces natural dopamine responsiveness, contributing to tolerance and escalation. The positive reinforcement loop theory accounts for escalating consumption.
  STRENGTH: Regression analysis with validated PPU measures; 2026 publication; specifically focuses on minor-onset viewers.
  STANCE: supported
  GAP: Cross-sectional; N=200 is modest; self-selected adult sample looking back retrospectively at adolescent use.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00787-019-01318-4
  DATE: 2019-03-27
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 775 female + 514 male Croatian high school students (mean age 15.9); 6-wave longitudinal study over ~2.5 years
  KEY_CLAIM: No significant longitudinal association between growth in pornography use and changes in self-esteem or symptoms of depression/anxiety for either sex; however, a significant negative baseline association between female adolescents' pornography use and psychological well-being was found at baseline.
  MECHANISM: Authors hypothesize that the cross-sectional associations found in other studies may reflect pre-existing individual differences rather than a causal effect of pornography exposure.
  STRENGTH: Six-wave longitudinal design with latent growth curve modeling; large representative sample; provides strong test of temporal ordering.
  STANCE: contested
  GAP: Middle-to-late adolescence only; Croatian sample; does not address effects of very early (pre-teen) exposure or internet pornography specifically vs general sexually explicit media.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6088458/
  DATE: 2018
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Two independent Croatian adolescent panel samples (N=455; N=858); cross-lagged path analysis
  KEY_CLAIM: Earlier pornography use was not consistently associated with subsequent decreases in subjective well-being for male adolescents in either panel; for female adolescents in one panel, higher baseline pornography use predicted lower well-being 12 months later, and lower well-being predicted increased pornography use — suggesting a bidirectional loop specifically for girls.
  MECHANISM: For a subgroup of female adolescents, pornography may contribute to body insecurities and self-esteem concerns, while simultaneously serving as an escape from low well-being — creating a reinforcing cycle.
  STRENGTH: Two independent samples; controlled for impulsiveness and family environment; 12-month follow-up.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Effects are gender-specific and sample-specific (one panel only for girls); does not address content-specific effects or age of first exposure.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31130200/
  DATE: 2019
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 248 male high school students (mean age 16.1); PROBIOPS study; 5-wave, 24-month longitudinal; content progression thesis test
  KEY_CLAIM: The "Content Progression Thesis" — that pornography use drives escalation to increasingly violent/coercive content through satiation — is NOT supported longitudinally; preference for violent/coercive pornography decreased over 24 months and was unrelated to changes in pornography use frequency.
  MECHANISM: No conditioning-based escalation to extreme content in adolescent males over 2 years; the assumption that regular exposure mechanically drives tolerance-based escalation to violence is empirically challenged.
  STRENGTH: First longitudinal test of CPT using dual-domain latent growth curve modeling; rigorous design.
  STANCE: contested (challenges the escalation narrative for adolescents specifically)
  GAP: Male adolescents only; 16-year-old mean age; does not address adult compulsive users; does not test escalation to fetish or extreme content outside violence/coercion.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10508-025-03217-z
  DATE: 2025-08-20
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: 664 adolescents aged 12–17, Galicia, Spain; cross-sectional survey
  KEY_CLAIM: 48.8% of adolescents surveyed had consumed pornography at least once; those who had used pornography scored significantly higher on traditional sexist attitudes (Cohen's d=0.29, p<0.001) and had 2–6x higher rates of risky online and offline sexual behaviors (e.g., sexting 6x higher).
  MECHANISM: Social learning theory: pornographic content provides sexual role models and contributes to development of sexual scripts; 3AM Theory (Acquisition, Activation, Application) explains how scripts migrate from screen to offline behavior.
  STRENGTH: Large age-appropriate sample; validated instruments; Spanish context adds cross-cultural data.
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Cross-sectional only — cannot determine whether pornography causes sexist attitudes or whether adolescents with existing sexist attitudes seek pornography.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13178-026-01290-0
  DATE: 2026-02-23
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Longitudinal study; adolescent media exposure data linked to adult pornography use; IMDb parental guide ratings used as exposure measure
  KEY_CLAIM: Adolescents who did NOT use pornography during adolescence but who watched mainstream media with moderate-to-severe sexual content were more likely to use pornography in adulthood; for those who already used pornography during adolescence, prior use (not mainstream media) was the dominant predictor of adult use.
  MECHANISM: Narrative transportation into sexualized media activates amygdala-mediated memory consolidation; repeated exposure normalizes sexual scripts and creates a feedback loop (selection + socialization); desensitization drives search for more explicit content.
  STRENGTH: Longitudinal design; novel use of IMDb parental guides as proxy measure; addresses a previously untested pathway (mainstream media to later pornography use).
  STANCE: mixed
  GAP: Relies on retrospective recall of media content; IMDb rating is an imperfect proxy for actual sexual content experienced.

- SOURCE_TYPE: peer_reviewed
  URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13178-022-00698-8.pdf
  DATE: 2023
  POPULATION/CONTEXT: Emerging adults (17–25 years) in a university sample; retrospective and cross-sectional design
  KEY_CLAIM: Majority of males recall first pornography exposure between ages 12–14; 28.2% of males and 23.7% of females report first exposure at ages 9–11; earlier exposure, higher viewing frequency, positive affective responses at initial exposure, sexual impulsivity, and IP-related sexual beliefs all independently predict self-assessed problematic pornography use.
  MECHANISM: Positive initial emotional responses (arousal, curiosity) to early exposure predict higher subsequent viewing frequency; IP-related beliefs (e.g., "porn is realistic") mediate relationship between exposure and problematic use; earlier single-male exposure is the highest-risk profile for developing PIP.
  STRENGTH: Assesses both cognitive expectancies and exposure factors simultaneously; UK emerging adult sample.
  STANCE: supported (for early exposure as risk factor for PPU)
  GAP: Retrospective recall of first-exposure age is subject to memory bias; cross-sectional; self-selected university sample.
