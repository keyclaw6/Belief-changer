## Failure autopsy

### Raw observation

The failure is not accurately described as three independent writer misses. R1 already attributed a toddler's comfort to attention rather than the sweet, said advertising's association arrived before the critical mind could question it, promised the reader was `"already halfway out"`, and said `"Nobody can take it back"`; its reviewer blocked other defects but did not flag those claims. It also missed the cross-chapter `"Why does this matter?"` repetition that the R2 reviewer later caught. R2 retained or strengthened the learning and permanence language, but its reviewer did not flag either. R3 repaired the cited cross-chapter duplicate, retained or expanded both unsupported claims, and introduced a second verbatim `"Maybe I am simply powerless around it"` occurrence. The R3 reviewer then blocked all three groups.

### Most likely mechanism

Two mechanisms are entangled:

1. **Review variance / false-negative exposure.** A fresh review can correctly reject an artifact yet still miss latent defects that survive into the next revision. The R1-to-R2 delayed repetition finding and the R2-to-R3 permanence/association findings provide two direct examples.
2. **Whole-artifact resampling regression.** Asking for another complete chapter to repair a narrow current review permits correct passages to be regenerated. R3 fixed the cited defects but expanded a missed unsupported mechanism and created a new duplicate.

The failing owner is therefore the generic writer-review handoff and revision mode, not a missing sugar fact or a Chapter 3-specific shared prompt rule. The plan already bounded evidence and assigned the one belief move; the current chapter can be repaired without changing either.

### Causal interpretation and signal risks

This run supports neither a permanent multi-reviewer gate nor a scoped-revision rule yet. The R2 and R3 wording was not identical, so the later reviewer may have reacted to R3's stronger formulation rather than simply correcting an R2 false negative. A future acceptance after a review-grounded edit could also be stochastic resampling rather than evidence for editing scope. Conversely, treating R3 as wholly bad would be a false negative: the review explicitly accepted most method, anatomy, continuity, and curve work.

A single repaired sugar chapter will remain product evidence only. No revision strategy becomes a shared factory default until it is replicated on another chapter and survives the untouched Stage C subject holdout.
