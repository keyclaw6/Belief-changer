# AUTHORITATIVE SEMANTIC COMMISSION — C-03

Commission Chapter 3, “A Choice Made Under a Story,” at approximately 2,600 words. Its sole belief move is to preserve the reality of the reader’s choices while exposing that those choices were made under unexamined assumptions about what BAD SUGAR would provide. Correcting an expected-payoff belief restores agency; it does not erase agency retrospectively. The chapter resolves three forms of resistance: “I choose it because I like it,” “I am powerless,” and “this book is just another form of brainwashing.”

BAD SUGAR retains the boundary already established under DEF-02 and DEF-03: the categorical core of sugar-sweetened drinks, confection, desserts, sweet baked goods, and added-sugar sweet snacks, with only genuinely ambiguous refined savoury snacks classified by whether they are acting as discretionary reward, rescue, graze, or automatic nibble. It does not include whole fruit, ordinary meal carbohydrates, small incidental ingredients, clinically directed glucose, accidents, or honest ambiguity. “Sugar,” “a sugar hit,” and “quitting sugar” mean only BAD SUGAR. Treat this definition as received understanding; do not reopen or reteach the classification work.

The story under examination is BC-03: **BAD SUGAR gives me a special pleasure, reward, comfort, or quick lift that ordinary food and ordinary life cannot provide, so removing it would leave me deprived.** This chapter does not yet decide each component of that claim. It establishes that liking, choosing, anticipating, saving, or feeling strongly pulled toward something cannot make the beliefs attached to it exempt from examination. Pleasant taste remains real. Emotion, hunger, social pleasure, and the right to choose remain real. What becomes provisional is the assumption that BAD SUGAR uniquely owns the expected payoff.

Debut M-03 exactly as `the sugar trap`. Here it means the whole learned decision-and-attribution structure: a familiar story assigns BAD SUGAR value, and choices are then made with that value already assumed. It is not a biological creature, an inner adversary, a diagnosis, or proof that choice was stolen. AN-13, the pre-filled order slip, carries this distinction: the reader still makes the choice, but inherited payoff assumptions may arrive already ticked; honest examination clears the form rather than taking the form away. Do not assign a universal source to those assumptions. In particular, do not claim that advertising created every belief or that any outside actor controlled a particular choice.

Echo M-01 exactly: `You can test whether BAD SUGAR owns any special or irreplaceable pleasure, without denying pleasant taste or giving up your right to choose.` Echo M-06 exactly as `anticipating, bargaining, and starting over`, using it only to recall the decision burden that makes examination worthwhile. The chapter’s own frozen instruction, I-03, must appear verbatim:

`QUESTION THIS BOOK AND THE OLD SUGAR STORY BY THE SAME STANDARD; KEEP ONLY WHAT SURVIVES HONEST EXAMINATION.`

This is the chapter’s meta-inoculation. The book asks for neither submission nor rebellion. Its claims receive no exemption from the scrutiny applied to the old sugar story. Holding the book, recognizing oneself in it, or questioning it proves neither incapacity nor gullibility. The reading-period contract remains in force: this chapter asks for no new restriction, compensation, or stopping decision, and qualified clinical instructions continue to outrank the method.

SC-02, the justification gallery, is the chapter’s main lived-material responsibility. Its job is recognition before demolition: allow the reader to hear the range of reasons assigned to BAD SUGAR without ridiculing those reasons or resolving their promised benefits. The gallery establishes the agenda that later benefit examination will answer; it must not perform that later examination itself.

Only EV-01, EV-02, and EV-11 are assigned as evidence:

EV-01 (S-004#E-001; S-009#E-001) supplies two bounded lived accounts: “There’s always room for dessert. If I go out for dinner, I have to look at the dessert menu before I order my mains,” and “I tried to cut down on soft drinks, but I start drinking them again for the taste.” These come from small qualitative samples—seven self-selected adults and nineteen students at one Saudi university. They permit the claims that some people describe taste as a reason for return and that dessert attention can begin before the main meal. They do not establish universal anticipation, imaginary taste, addiction, physiological need, or one universal decision mechanism.

EV-02 (S-022#E-001, S-022#E-002, S-022#E-003, S-022#E-005; S-020#E-005) supplies participant associations between sweets and reward, deserving, weakness, stress, nervousness, sadness, boredom, loneliness, and social occasions, together with one report of eating an extra slice after tennis and afternoon tea despite not being hungry and attributing it to taste. These are reports from particular participants and communities. They permit the inference that BAD SUGAR is assigned multiple sensory, emotional, social, and ceremonial jobs. They do not show that those jobs share one cause, that weakness meant sugar deficiency, that emotional eating is universal, or that an association proves efficacy or mechanism. “Weakness” remains the participant’s attribution; persistent or medically relevant weakness is not to be reinterpreted as a belief problem.

EV-11 (S-008#E-001) supplies the lived analogy, “people get addicted to drugs, some of us are addicted to soda.” It permits acknowledgment that some readers use addiction language to describe felt compulsion. It supplies no clinical diagnosis, drug equivalence, mechanism, or proof of powerlessness. Do not adjudicate addiction science here and do not use the absence of a diagnosis to dismiss genuine difficulty.

Use RV-02, RV-10, and RV-14 as source-grounded reader recognition rather than as research findings: “I go back for the taste”; “I am not eating it, but I am saving it for the person I will be when this is over”; and “Maybe I am simply powerless around it.” RV-02 voices the taste defense without proving unique value. RV-10 exposes preserved reward fantasy without licensing a claim that all abstinence causes backlash. RV-14 makes room for the felt experience of powerlessness without endorsing a powerless identity.

Keep the curve at D2/F1. Demolition is active only at the level of unquestioned assumptions; freedom appears as the possibility of clearer, reader-owned choice. Do not yet reassign taste, reward, relief, accomplishment, celebration, belonging, or care to their sources. Do not explain an anticipation–consumption mechanism, why restrictive methods create conflict, the science of addiction or withdrawal, commercial optimization, planned-exception logic, or the eventual stopping threshold. Supply no exercise, diet, replacement system, trigger-avoidance strategy, purity practice, health warning, or outcome promise.

The chapter must never shame, diagnose, moralize at, or order the reader; describe an inner creature; say choice was stolen; treat the reader as weak-willed; or imply that a chosen managed approach is illegitimate. Clinical and eating-disorder care remain outside and above this inquiry. The chapter’s handoff is complete when the reader can hold both truths at once: “I chose” and “the payoff assumptions behind that choice can still be tested.” C-04 receives a reader willing to examine each claimed benefit rather than treating choice itself as proof that the benefit is unique or necessary.
