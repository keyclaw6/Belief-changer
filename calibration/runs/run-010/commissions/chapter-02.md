# AUTHORITATIVE SEMANTIC COMMISSION — C-02

C-02, working title “Draw the Line, Free the Rest,” owns one belief move: a precise behavioral target makes practical totality compatible with ordinary eating and a non-catastrophic margin. It resolves the fear that quitting sugar must spread into fear of fruit, carbohydrates, hidden ingredients, mistakes, or food generally. Its curve is D1 / F1: enough correction to end category confusion and offer relief, without beginning the later demolition of sugar’s claimed benefits. Budget: 3,000 words.

The reader arrives having begun no new restriction and having permission to investigate without an immediate stopping decision. Address especially the informed household translator who knows labels but still lacks a livable line (R-04/RV-08), the restriction veteran who expects a boundary to become food-wide prohibition (R-02), and the decision-tax escapee who wants the debate closed rather than every molecule policed (R-06). RV-13 supplies the specific fear that if fruit counts as “sugar,” the boundary has no natural end. Hand C-03 a target that is clear enough that it cannot later expand or contract opportunistically.

The target remains the compulsive decision loop around BAD SUGAR: anticipating it, assigning it jobs, negotiating access, consuming it as reward or rescue, and resetting afterward. It is not molecular purity, universal carbohydrate restriction, weight loss, diabetes treatment, or dietary perfection. The eventual destination is no planned BAD SUGAR doses and no continuing sugar debate, but this chapter does not tell the reader to cross that threshold now.

Own DEF-01 through DEF-06 in full.

GOOD SUGAR (DEF-01) is a method label for sugars and starches found in ordinary nourishment: whole fruit, vegetables, legumes, nuts, plain dairy, and ordinary meal staples including oats, rice, potatoes, bread, and pasta. These foods are outside the target. “Good” is neither a moral judgment nor a claim of biochemical superiority.

BAD SUGAR (DEF-02) has a categorical core and one behavior-resolved edge. The core consists of products conventionally consumed as sugar-sweetened drinks, confection, desserts, sweet baked goods, or added-sugar sweet snacks, whether sweetened with sugar, honey, or syrup. Core examples include soda, sugar-sweetened coffee or tea, sweets, biscuits, cakes, pastries, ice cream, dessert products, and prominently sweetened cereals or bars. Hunger, homemade status, care, celebration, convenience, or labels such as natural, organic, dark, low-sugar, or healthier cannot move a core item outside the category.

The gray savoury edge applies only to a genuinely ambiguous refined savoury snack. Such an item enters BAD SUGAR when it is functioning as a discretionary reward, rescue, graze, or automatic nibble rather than ordinary meal food. The meal-or-hit inquiry may bring an edge item inside the line; it may never exempt a core sweet item. Small incidental ingredients in an ordinary meal do not turn that meal into BAD SUGAR. The definition does not claim that every item inside BAD SUGAR has identical physiology.

Under DEF-03, “sugar,” “a sugar hit,” and “quitting sugar” mean BAD SUGAR as defined here. They do not mean fruit, ordinary meals, every carbohydrate, or ingredient-level purity.

DEF-04 establishes totality only within that line. Moderation remains a legal, moral, and practical option, and the reader retains the right to choose. Recurring planned exceptions are not presented as desire-level freedom because they preserve the proposition that selected moments need a sugar upgrade. State that boundary without undertaking the later argument against individual exception strategies.

DEF-05 is the Molecule Margin. A trace ingredient, uncertain restaurant sauce, accidental bite, or genuine classification mistake is not a deliberate return and does not constitute failure. The reader may notice it, learn from it if useful, and continue from the choice already made. The margin covers accidents and honest ambiguity; it neither disguises a planned exception nor requires ingredient surveillance.

DEF-06 protects whole fruit and ordinary meal carbohydrates from category creep. One hundred percent juice, dried fruit, non-nutritive sweeteners, alcohol, caffeine, and adjacent indulgences are not required quits and do not define success. The reader may examine any of them later if desired, but that optional inquiry may neither enlarge this book’s target nor create a consolation regime.

Debut the Ordinary Eating Compass (LX-P01) as temporary positive authority: it returns authority to ordinary nourishment and actual sources of pleasure and succeeds by eventually becoming unnecessary. Introduce only the assigned instruments here. The meal-or-hit check (LX-P02) is confined to the genuinely gray savoury edge and cannot excuse a core item. The Molecule Margin (LX-P04) is an interpretive principle, never an ingredient-monitoring practice.

PX-03 is a one-time boundary sort using a few representative examples across four categories: categorical core, protected ordinary food, genuine gray savoury edge, and Molecule Margin. Its sole job is to teach the distinction once. It must not become recurring homework, scoring, label scanning, meal-by-meal classification, or a permanent dashboard.

SC-07 should preserve the meaning of household care while showing that labelled, homemade, natural, or ostensibly better products remain subject to the same categorical definition. AN-08, the label costume rack, makes clear that changed ingredients or presentation do not move a core sweet item outside BAD SUGAR. AN-09, the customs desk for molecules, shows how ingredient perfection creates endless border checks and how the Molecule Margin restores an operational boundary. These devices explain the distinction; they are not biomedical evidence.

EV-10 is assigned only for household and label context. One caregiver reported changing a child’s drinks and checking labels; another shopper distrusted commercial drinks and used a homemade alternative that could not be assumed sugar-free (S-010#E-001; S-011#E-001). The permitted inference is that knowledge can affect household choices and that homemade or better-labelled status does not supersede the category. Do not infer caregiver abstinence, child-health outcomes, moral superiority, or automatic freedom through substitution.

EV-17 is assigned only to prevent the slogan that all carbohydrate sources and exposures are interchangeable. Reviews concerning substantial fructose or sugar-sweetened-beverage exposure and a controlled ultra-processed-food trial show that source, dose, and product context matter; the trial did not isolate sugar as the responsible feature (S-002#E-001; S-019#E-001). Some studied exposures were unusually high or hypercaloric. Do not infer guaranteed metabolic harm or repair, fruit-soda equivalence, sugar as the isolated cause of the trial’s outcomes, or a health-based reason to quit. Any health fact must be immediately detached from fear, diagnosis, treatment advice, or promised benefit.

Because C-02 owns SAFE-01 through SAFE-03, its boundary must visibly defer to clinical and eating-disorder safety. Medical glucose, prescribed nutrition, diabetes or metabolic treatment, pregnancy-related dietary care, and all other qualified clinical instructions sit outside the method and outrank it. The chapter may help an adult examine meanings assigned to discretionary sugar; it may not alter treatment, classify prescribed glucose as a lapse, or interpret persistent weakness or medically relevant symptoms as a belief problem.

The method is not eating-disorder treatment. If its categories or the boundary exercise increase food fear, compulsive checking, bingeing, purging, compensation, medically unsafe restriction, or escalating distress, the reader is to pause and seek qualified care. BAD SUGAR must never become a moral-contamination category. Do not prescribe fasting, compensation, calorie targets, body checking, disgust, a ceremonial final dose, a last-supper binge, or accidental-exposure purity rituals, and do not present the method as a substitute for care.

The primary reader is an adult examining their own decision loop. Household material may model calm definitions and non-moral language, but it may not prescribe a child’s diet, create guilt about a child’s body, or override pediatric or other qualified advice.

Debut M-09 exactly as frozen:

`You can close the daily sugar debate without policing every molecule, fighting yourself, or treating one accidental bite as failure.`

Reproduce the owning instruction I-02 verbatim:

`USE BAD SUGAR ONLY FOR THE DEFINED TARGET; DO NOT EXPAND IT TO WHOLE FRUIT, ORDINARY MEALS, OR EVERY CARBOHYDRATE, AND LET QUALIFIED CLINICAL ADVICE OVERRIDE THIS BOOK.`

Keep agency intact. “Good” and “bad” are never moral identities; ordinary food remains ordinary food; an accident is not failure; and the chapter offers no replacement diet, purity identity, trigger-avoidance system, or command to stop. Do not explain the learned decision mechanism, test sugar’s claimed special benefits, litigate exception strategies, diagnose addiction, promise health outcomes, or construct a post-threshold lapse protocol. This chapter’s work is complete when the reader has one stable, usable line that protects both practical totality and the rest of ordinary eating.
