## Target behavior

Compulsive consumption of refined or added sugar and junk-carbohydrate products inside the operational category `BAD SUGAR`, especially the craving, anticipation, bargaining, reward, rescue, exception, lapse, and reset loop.

The target is not eating, carbohydrates, nutrition management, body weight, diabetes treatment, molecular purity, or every pleasant taste.

## Primary reader and personas

The book addresses one general adult reader through six functional personas:

1. The Sweet-Reward Reader
2. The Restriction Veteran
3. The Context Carrier
4. The Informed Household Translator
5. The Managed-Truce Maintainer
6. The Decision-Tax Escapee

The household persona incorporates the caregiver evidence without claiming that the caregiver personally achieved sugar freedom.

## Load-bearing false belief

**BAD SUGAR gives me a special pleasure, reward, comfort, or quick lift that ordinary food and ordinary life cannot provide, so removing it would leave me deprived.**

The argument denies unique ownership, irreplaceability, and necessity. It does not deny pleasant taste, calories, distress, hunger, celebration, or the reader’s reported experience.

## Redefinition decision

### GOOD SUGAR

Sugars and starches occurring in ordinary nourishment, including whole fruit, vegetables, legumes, nuts, plain dairy, oats, rice, potatoes, bread, pasta, and other ordinary meal staples.

### BAD SUGAR

Either:

1. A sugar-sweetened drink, confection, dessert, sweet baked good, or added-sugar sweet snack, whether sweetened with sugar, honey, or syrup; or
2. A genuinely ambiguous refined savoury snack functioning as a discretionary reward, rescue, graze, or automatic nibble rather than ordinary meal food.

The behavioral-role inquiry applies only to the gray refined-savoury edge. It cannot exempt a core sweet drink, confection, dessert, baked sweet, or sweet snack.

Small incidental ingredients in an ordinary meal do not transform that meal into BAD SUGAR.

### Definitional decree

**Throughout this book, whenever I say sugar, a sugar hit, or quitting sugar, take me to mean BAD SUGAR as defined here—not fruit, ordinary meals, or every carbohydrate.**

### Totality inside the line

The destination is no planned BAD SUGAR doses. This is practical total freedom, not molecular purity and not a loss of legal or moral autonomy. Moderation remains possible, but recurring negotiations and exceptions are not renamed desire-level freedom.

### Margin for error

`the Molecule Margin` protects accidents, incidental ingredients, uncertain restaurant sauces, an accidental bite, and honest classification mistakes from being converted into panic, failure, or permission creep.

It does not protect a planned exception disguised after the fact as an accident.

### Conditional exclusions

One hundred percent juice, dried fruit, non-nutritive sweeteners, alcohol, caffeine, and adjacent indulgences are outside the required quit. They may be examined later without altering the book’s success criterion.

## Medical and eating-disorder perimeter

Medical glucose, prescribed nutrition, diabetes or metabolic treatment, pregnancy-related dietary advice, persistent weakness, and eating-disorder care are outside the belief method. Qualified clinical instructions outrank the book.

The book supplies no medication changes, glucose targets, calorie targets, carbohydrate prescription, weight-loss mechanics, crisis treatment, or withdrawal schedule.

# 2. Book-level through-line

The reader arrives wanting the sugar debate to stop but expecting that stopping means losing a pleasure. The book first narrows the target so that food, fruit, ordinary meals, medicine, and accidental molecules are not threatened. It then shows that the reader’s choices were made inside a learned valuation system: `the sweet-reward script` opens anticipation, and eating may quiet the current episode while receiving credit for far more than its taste supplied. Each claimed benefit is returned to its real source—appetite, accomplishment, rest, company, care, convenience, or the end of restriction—without denying the source or insulting the reader. The book distinguishes documented commercial optimization from ordinary availability, and subjective difficulty from unsupported claims of universal addiction or withdrawal. It then exposes `the deprivation method`, managed truce, planned exceptions, purity, and powerless identity as different ways of keeping BAD SUGAR important. After a bounded testimony and an explicit challenge to the book’s own claims, the reader audits what they now understand, chooses a threshold without waiting for a ceremonial date, and receives a relapse-proof interpretation of thoughts, accidents, and social exposure. The closing movement hands over a quiet identity: the daily negotiation is finished, ordinary life remains, and freedom is the absence of anything left to settle.

# 13. Writer-level execution rules

2. Reproduce every assigned mantra exactly. Never paraphrase, recase, repunctuate, or invent one.
3. Invoke established concepts by token rather than repeating their original argument.
4. Previews, summaries, numbered instructions, and frozen mantras are the only licensed verbatim recap zones.
5. Keep questions at roughly 8–10% of sentences.
6. Keep roughly 20% of sentences under eight words, clustered around argumentative peaks.
7. Aim for 25–33 instances of direct `you/your` per 1,000 words.
8. Use `we` for entering and inhabiting the trap, `you` for investigation and escape, and `I` only for the book’s promise, warning, or methodological transparency.
9. Use at least one assigned concrete analogy or scene in every chapter.
10. Use one killer-line pair at most per major argument.
11. Scientific grades and limitations are not hedging defects. Core interpretive distinctions may be stated confidently; empirical uncertainty must remain uncertainty.
12. Do not fabricate an author biography, first-person sugar history, method pedigree, success rate, composite quotation, clinical authority conflict, or research result.
13. Do not use source quotations assigned to another chapter. Everything except frozen mantras and licensed recap language appears verbatim only once.
14. Keep the main prose emotionally clean. Detailed claim-and-limit treatment belongs in Chapter 19.
15. Do not let the final movement become a dietary system. The Compass and its audits must visibly retire.
