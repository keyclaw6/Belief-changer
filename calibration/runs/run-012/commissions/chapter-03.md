# AUTHORITATIVE SEMANTIC COMMISSION — C-03

Working title: **A Choice Made Under a Story**. Target length: 2,600 words. The chapter operates at D2 / F1: active but incomplete belief demolition, with only a glimpse of freedom.

C-03 owns one belief move: the reader’s BAD SUGAR choices were real, but those choices were evaluated through preloaded assumptions about what BAD SUGAR would provide. Examining and correcting those assumptions restores agency; it does not erase choice, excuse conduct as involuntary, or replace one controlling authority with another.

The chapter receives BAD SUGAR as an already settled method category under DEF-02 and DEF-03. It must not reopen or opportunistically expand that definition. “Sugar” here means the defined core of sweet drinks, confection, desserts, sweet baked goods, and added-sugar sweet snacks, plus only the behavior-resolved gray savoury edge. It does not mean whole fruit, ordinary meals, every carbohydrate, or molecular purity. “Good” and “bad” remain non-moral method labels.

“I choose it because I like it” must be treated as partly true but not self-validating. The choice occurred, and pleasant taste may be real. Neither fact proves that every larger payoff credited to BAD SUGAR—reward, rescue, completion, energy, comfort, or belonging—has been accurately understood. A stated reason for choosing is evidence of the reason the person currently accepts, not proof that the product uniquely delivers everything contained in that reason.

The chapter must remove shame without removing responsibility. The reader was not weak, defective, or secretly incapable merely because an option appeared valuable under the assumptions available at the time. Choices made under mistaken payoff beliefs remain choices. Honest examination clears those assumptions and reopens the choice. Do not claim that choice was stolen, that the reader was controlled, or that holding this book proves incapacity.

Debut M-03 exactly as `the sugar trap`. Here it means the whole learned decision-and-attribution structure in which BAD SUGAR receives advance credit for valued outcomes. It is not a biological creature, a second self, an attacking appetite, or a settled pharmacological mechanism. Do not personify it. This chapter names the structure but does not yet supply the later full account of anticipatory activation and closure.

AN-13, the pre-filled order slip, carries the agency argument. Its sole job is to show that choice can remain genuine even when inherited or rehearsed payoff assumptions arrive already ticked. Honest examination clears the form; it does not tear it up or hand it to the book. Do not use the analogy to imply deception by a specific actor, unconscious compulsion, or loss of legal or moral agency.

SC-02, the justification gallery, lets the reader hear recognizable reasons before later chapters test them. It should make visible the range of jobs currently assigned to BAD SUGAR: taste, dessert completion, reward and deserving, weakness or quick energy, relief from distress or emptiness, social participation, and felt powerlessness. These are claims on the demolition movement’s agenda, not conclusions to refute here. Do not turn the gallery into a fabricated composite testimony, a universal psychology, or an explanation that all reasons share one cause.
