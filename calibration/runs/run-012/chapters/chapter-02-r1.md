## WHY THE LINE MATTERS

In the last chapter we agreed to investigate. Not to restrict, not to start a plan — just to look honestly at whether BAD SUGAR delivers the pleasure and rescue you've always believed it does. That investigation starts with a question so practical it may seem beneath a book about freedom: *what, exactly, are we talking about?*

Ask most people what "quitting sugar" means, and watch the confusion arrive. Does it mean no fruit? No bread? No ketchup on the plate your child just handed you? Does honey count? What about a date bar? Is rice secretly the enemy?

Here is what the confusion does: it makes the project feel enormous before you've taken a single step. If "sugar" means every carbohydrate, then quitting sugar means quitting food — and no honest person can quit food. The target expands until it becomes the entire horizon, and at that point you haven't been given a challenge. You've been given a reason to stop looking.

Have you ever tried to tidy a room where you couldn't tell what belonged and what didn't? You pick up an item, hesitate, put it down, pick up another. Two hours later the room looks the same and you're exhausted — not from the work, but from the decisions. Now picture the same room with two clearly labeled boxes. Suddenly you can move. The task hasn't shrunk, but the doubt has.

That is what this chapter offers. Two labels. One clear line. And the freedom to stop interrogating every mouthful.

## TWO LABELS, NO MORAL JUDGMENT

From this point forward, when this book says "sugar," "a sugar hit," or "quitting sugar," it means one specific target — what we'll call **BAD SUGAR**. And everything else that contains sugars or starches in the ordinary course of nourishment belongs to a second category: **GOOD SUGAR**.

Let me be direct about what these labels are and what they are not. "Good" does not mean virtuous. "Bad" does not mean sinful. These are method labels — handles for two different kinds of eating — and they carry no more moral weight than "incoming" and "outgoing" on a filing cabinet. We are not dividing the world into clean and dirty. We are not ranking anyone's character by what sits on their plate. We are drawing a practical line so that the investigation we began in Chapter 1 can point at something real.

Why bother with labels at all? Because an unnamed target drifts. It expands on anxious days and contracts on lenient ones. It becomes whatever the latest headline says it is. A named target stays put. You can examine it, test your beliefs about it, and eventually — if the investigation persuades you — walk away from it without accidentally walking away from dinner.

## THE CATEGORICAL CORE: WHAT IS ALWAYS INSIDE THE LINE

BAD SUGAR has a center that doesn't move. These are products so clearly inside the target that no scenario, no label, and no occasion can pull them out:

**Sugar-sweetened drinks.** Soda, sweet iced tea, sugar-sweetened coffee drinks, fruit-flavoured drinks with added sugar, and any beverage whose sweetness comes from sugar, honey, or syrup.

**Confectionery.** Sweets, chocolate bars, candy of every kind.

**Dessert.** Ice cream, cake, pastries, pies, puddings, sweet baked goods of every description.

**Sweet snacks.** Biscuits, cookies, doughnuts, sweet cereal bars, and prominently sweetened breakfast cereals.

That's the center. Notice what unites them: they are conventionally consumed *as* sweet things. Nobody mistakes a slice of cake for lunch. Nobody opens a packet of sweets expecting a savoury experience. These items exist to deliver sweetness, and they deliver it by design.

Now, can you make a birthday cake from scratch, using high-quality butter and organic cane sugar, with your own hands and genuine care? Of course you can. Does the homemade status move it outside the line? It does not. Can the label say "raw," "natural," "lightly sweetened," "refined-sugar-free," or "made with honey"? It can say whatever it likes. A confection in a costume is still a confection. Does a special occasion — a wedding, a holiday, a child's first birthday — reclassify the product? The occasion changes the meaning of the gathering. It does not change what is in the bowl.

This may seem rigid. It isn't. It is the opposite of rigid — it is the thing that prevents rigidity from creeping into the rest of your eating, which is exactly where we're headed next.

## THE LABEL COSTUME RACK

Imagine a costume rack backstage at a theatre. Hanging on it you'll find a lab coat labeled "organic," a vintage apron labeled "homemade," a straw hat labeled "all-natural," a silk scarf labeled "refined-sugar-free (made with agave)." The costumes are real. They're well-made. Some of them are even lovely.

But when you pull them aside, standing behind the rack is the same performer: a product formulated to deliver a concentrated sweet hit. It may be a kinder version. It may be a more expensive version. It may taste better or worse. None of that matters for the purpose of this investigation, because the question we are asking is not "Is this particular formulation harmful?" The question is: "Does this product sit inside the pattern of eating I'm examining — the planned sugar hit, the anticipated reward, the sweet rescue — or doesn't it?"

If it does, the costume doesn't help. A cupcake baked at home with love and unrefined muscovado sugar is still a cupcake. A bottle of organic lemonade sweetened with raw honey is still a sugar-sweetened drink. These are not bad things. They are not moral failures. They are simply inside the line we've drawn, because the line follows the behavior and the product category, not the ingredient list.

Why does this matter so much? Because every loophole you create in a boundary becomes the hole the old pattern walks through. Not because you're weak — because the pattern is resourceful. It can dress itself in any label that lets it keep operating. Drawing the line at the category, not the ingredient, takes the costume rack out of the game entirely.

## ORDINARY FOOD STAYS ORDINARY

Here is the promise this chapter exists to make, and I want you to hear it clearly:

**Whole fruit, vegetables, legumes, nuts, plain dairy, oats, rice, potatoes, bread, and pasta are not BAD SUGAR. They are not the target. They are not under investigation. They are ordinary food, and they remain ordinary.**

Does a banana contain sugar? Of course it does. Does a bowl of rice convert to glucose in your body? Naturally — that is part of what it's for. Is this book about to tell you that an apple is a threat, that a baked potato is a dose, that you need to count the sugars in a carrot? It is not. If that were true, the word "sugar" would mean everything edible, and a book called "quitting sugar" would be a book about starvation.

There is a real and reasonable fear behind the question "If fruit is sugar too, where could the line possibly end?" The answer is: it ends here. Right now. In this chapter. We are drawing the line, and the line protects everything you eat as ordinary nourishment. Fruit is not the enemy. Your lunchtime sandwich is not the enemy. A plate of pasta is not the enemy. These foods sit comfortably outside the target, and they will stay there for the rest of this book.

You may have encountered approaches that do expand the target — programs that ask you to measure net carbs in a peach, or that treat a bowl of porridge with suspicion. This is not one of them. The investigation we're conducting is about a specific pattern of eating: the planned sugar hit, the anticipated sweet reward, the familiar rescue. Ordinary meals are not that pattern, and treating them as though they were would make the project intolerable for no good reason.

I mention this because science sometimes delivers findings about sugar metabolism that are worth understanding but easy to misapply. For instance, a 2020 narrative review by Softic and colleagues in *Critical Reviews in Clinical Laboratory Sciences* examined how substantial fructose exposure — particularly from sugar-sweetened beverages consumed in large quantities — may be connected to hepatic insulin resistance through several proposed pathways. Some cited interventions used unusually high or hypercaloric exposures.

What matters for us is not the biochemistry but the principle the biochemistry supports: *source, dose, and exposure context matter*. Eating an orange is not the same event as drinking a litre of orange soda, even though both contain fructose. The context is different. The dose is different. The eating pattern is different. This is exactly why the line exists — to honour those differences, not to erase them. I am not asking you to use this finding to be frightened. I am asking you to notice that it confirms the value of a clear target: ordinary nourishment is not the target, and the science does not make it so.

## THE GENUINE GRAY EDGE

You may be thinking: what about a savoury snack? A packet of salted crisps? A handful of crackers? These aren't sweet. They aren't conventionally a dessert or a confection. But they're also not exactly dinner.

This is the one place where the line genuinely gets blurry — not in sweet territory, where the classification is categorical, but at the refined savoury edge. And here I want to introduce a simple instrument that belongs to what we'll call the **Ordinary Eating Compass** — a temporary teaching tool that exists to restore ordinary nourishment to its proper place and to help you navigate genuine uncertainty. The Compass is not a permanent monitoring system. It is not a scorecard. It carries one boundary instrument, it does one job, and it is designed to become unnecessary once the job is done.

The instrument is this: the **meal-or-hit check**. When you encounter a genuinely ambiguous refined savoury item — not a sweet product, not a piece of fruit, not a plate of dinner, but something in the genuine gray zone — you ask one question: *Is this functioning as ordinary eating, or is it functioning as a discretionary reward, a rescue, a graze, or an automatic nibble?*

A handful of crisps that you pulled from the cupboard at 3 p.m. because you were bored and restless, eaten standing at the counter without hunger — that's functioning as a hit. The same crisps served alongside a sandwich at lunch — that's functioning as part of a meal. The item is the same. The role is different. And the role is what moves a genuinely ambiguous savoury item inside or outside the line.

Two things this check cannot do. It cannot move any categorical-core item outside the line — no amount of role inquiry turns a slice of cake into a meal. And it cannot turn ordinary food into a suspect — a baked potato is a baked potato regardless of when you eat it. The check exists solely for the narrow band of genuine ambiguity, and it answers the question by looking at what the eating is *doing*, not by inspecting an ingredient panel.

## THE MOLECULE MARGIN

Now for the fear that lives beneath all of this — the one that makes people abandon sensible targets before they begin. It sounds like this: "What about the sugar in the ketchup? What about the sauce at the restaurant? What about the teaspoon of honey in the stir-fry dressing? If I can't know every ingredient, how can I possibly draw a line?"

Picture a customs desk at an airport. The customs officer's job is to intercept prohibited items — large, identifiable, clearly belonging in one category. Now imagine a customs officer who insisted on testing every passenger's clothing fibres, analysing every molecule of dust on their shoes, demanding a chemical report on the dye in their shoelaces. That officer would never clear a single traveller. The queue would stretch to the horizon. The airport would cease to function — not because security failed, but because perfection made the system impossible.

Ingredient-level purity would do the same thing to your eating. It would turn every meal into a border check and every restaurant visit into an investigation. And it would guarantee exactly the kind of exhausting surveillance that we identified in Chapter 1 as the problem — the endless anticipating, bargaining, and starting over.

So here is the principle, and I want you to hold it like a key: **the Molecule Margin**. A trace ingredient in a savoury sauce, an uncertain dressing at a restaurant, an accidental bite of something at a party that turns out to contain sugar you didn't expect — none of these is a planned dose. None is a deliberate return. None is a failure of any kind.

You can close the daily sugar debate without policing every molecule, fighting yourself, or treating one accidental bite as failure.

Read that line again. It is the operating promise of this entire approach. The Molecule Margin means that honest accidents remain honest accidents. You notice them, you learn from them if there's something useful to learn, and you continue from the choice you've already made. The only thing the margin does not cover is the attempt to disguise a planned exception as an accident. You'll know the difference. You always do.

## A ONE-TIME SORT

Let me show you how this works in practice — not as a repeated exercise, not as a logging habit, but as a single demonstration to make the principle visible. We'll sort a few examples, once, and then we'll stop.

**Categorical core (always BAD SUGAR):** A can of cola. A chocolate biscuit. A slice of birthday cake — yes, even the one your colleague made at home. A tub of ice cream labeled "all-natural." A packet of sweets from a health-food shop sweetened with coconut sugar. A bowl of prominently sweetened breakfast cereal.

**Protected ordinary food (never BAD SUGAR):** An apple. A bowl of rice. A baked potato with butter. A handful of almonds. A glass of milk. A plate of pasta with tomato sauce. A piece of toast. A banana in your child's lunchbox.

**Genuine gray savoury edge (use the meal-or-hit check):** A bag of salted crisps. A handful of savoury crackers. A refined savoury snack from the vending machine. Ask: is this part of a meal, or is it functioning as a reward, a rescue, or a mindless graze? The answer places it.

**Molecule Margin:** The sugar in the bread. The honey in a salad dressing at a restaurant. The dash of something sweet in a stir-fry sauce you didn't make. These are not doses. These are not decisions. These are traces, and they do not move.

That's the sort. You don't need to do it again. The principle is the teacher. Once you've seen it work, the categories are obvious in any kitchen, any menu, any shop aisle.

## ONE CAREGIVER, ONE SHOPPER, AND THE LINE BETWEEN CARE AND CATEGORY

Let me share two accounts that illustrate why the line must follow the product, not the story around it.

In a 2025 study published in the *Maternal and Child Health Journal*, researchers interviewed caregivers about changes they made after receiving nutritional guidance. One parent described a shift: "I started giving her water more. I started changing up her drinks. I started checking the sugars on the back." What strikes me about this is the clarity of the response. Information arrived; behavior shifted. She didn't need willpower. She didn't need a complex plan. She looked at the product, saw something she hadn't noticed before, and made a different choice. Information can open a door.

In a separate study — a 2026 qualitative study published in the *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health* — a shopper in Ghana described avoiding commercial sweet beverages because of ingredient distrust and instead making homemade sobolo, a traditional hibiscus drink. The distrust was real. The care was real. The homemade drink was made with genuine attention.

But here is the point this chapter exists to make: the researchers themselves noted that traditional homemade drinks may contain unrecognised sugar. The homemade label — the love, the local tradition, the real ingredients — didn't change what was in the glass.

These are two different people, in two different countries, making two different choices. I am not comparing them or fusing their stories. I'm pointing to the same principle from two angles. The first account shows that information about what's in a product can genuinely change behavior — that looking honestly at the label is worth doing. The second shows that care, tradition, and homemade status, real and admirable as they are, cannot redraw the line. A sweet drink is a sweet drink, whether it came from a factory or from your grandmother's kitchen.

This is not a judgment of either person. It is not a judgment of anyone's cooking or tradition or care. It is a simple operational fact: the category follows the product, not the presentation. And that is precisely what protects you from the costume rack.

## WHAT THIS BOOK DOES NOT ASK YOU TO QUIT

Before we move on, let me close a door that shouldn't have been open.

One hundred percent fruit juice. Dried fruit. Non-nutritive sweeteners. Alcohol. Caffeine. Adjacent indulgences of any kind. This book does not ask you to quit any of them. They are not part of the target. They do not define success or failure in this investigation. If you want to examine your relationship with any of them, you're welcome to do so — separately, on your own terms, with appropriate guidance. But this investigation has one target, and expanding it would be an act of anxiety, not an act of clarity.

The Ordinary Eating Compass exists to protect this boundary as much as to enforce it. Its job is to say: *this is ordinary; leave it alone. This is the target; look at it honestly.* The Compass succeeds when you no longer need it — when the categories are so obvious that you don't consult anything at all.

## THE DESTINATION INSIDE THE LINE

Now that we can see the target, let me name the destination this investigation points toward — not to demand you walk there today, but so you know where the road goes.

The destination is: **no planned BAD SUGAR doses**. Not fewer. Not smaller. Not only on weekdays. No planned ones.

Does that sound severe? It may. But consider the alternative. Moderation — a few planned exceptions, special-occasion allowances, a controlled weekly treat — is a legitimate choice. It is a morally and legally and practically valid way to live. This book does not deny that. What this book does say, and what the rest of the investigation will examine in detail, is that recurring planned exceptions carry a cost that isn't always visible: they preserve the proposition that selected moments *need* a sugar upgrade. They keep the debate open. They maintain the very negotiation that brought you here.

We will explore this more fully in later chapters. For now, notice just the shape of it: the destination of no planned doses is not a punishment. It is the only position from which the debate genuinely ends. Where there are no planned exceptions, there is nothing to anticipate, nothing to bargain over, nothing to recover from.

Moderation keeps the conversation running. Closure ends it. The investigation ahead will let you see which one you actually want.

And the Molecule Margin means that closure does not require perfection. A trace doesn't reopen anything. An honest accident doesn't reopen anything. Only a planned return does. That is the simplicity this chapter has been building toward: a target you can see, a margin you can live with, and an ordinary life of food that remains exactly, peacefully, ordinary.

## A SAFETY NOTE

Before you turn the page, there are situations this book cannot and does not address.

If you take prescribed glucose or follow any form of medically directed nutrition — for diabetes, for metabolic treatment, during pregnancy, or for any other clinical reason — those instructions sit outside this method entirely and outrank it. Prescribed glucose is not a lapse. Persistent weakness is not a mistaken belief to be corrected by re-reading a chapter. If in doubt, consult your physician or dietitian, and follow their guidance, not this book's.

And if at any point the categories in this chapter — or the one-time sort, or the Molecule Margin — increase food fear, compulsive checking, bingeing, purging, compensatory behavior, medically unsafe restriction, or escalating distress, **stop and seek qualified care**. This investigation is not eating-disorder treatment. It is not designed for that purpose, and its categories are not calibrated for that context. If the tool is causing harm, put it down and get the right help.

That said: for the reader who simply wants to examine whether BAD SUGAR delivers the pleasure it promises — without surveillance, without extremism, without a lifetime of rules — you now have a target you can hold in one hand.

## 2ND INSTRUCTION: USE BAD SUGAR ONLY FOR THE DEFINED TARGET; DO NOT EXPAND IT TO WHOLE FRUIT, ORDINARY MEALS, OR EVERY CARBOHYDRATE, AND LET QUALIFIED CLINICAL ADVICE OVERRIDE THIS BOOK.

This is the guardrail that protects everything we've built today. The line can't help you if it drifts. Fix it here. BAD SUGAR is the categorical core: the sweet drinks, the confectionery, the desserts, the sweet snacks. Ordinary nourishment is outside. The Molecule Margin covers the traces. The Ordinary Eating Compass keeps the boundary visible until the boundary keeps itself. And if a qualified professional tells you something different about your body, their voice overrides mine.

Hold the line where it is. Not tighter. Not wider. Exactly here. Ordinary food is free to remain ordinary, and the target is too clearly drawn to escape through a costume or a calendar.
