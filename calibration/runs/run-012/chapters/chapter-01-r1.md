## THE ARGUMENT YOU ALREADY KNOW

You've picked up a book about sugar. Which means a voice in you already suspects what a different voice has been arguing against for months — maybe years. One side says *this has to change*. The other side says *not yet, not today, not like that*.

If I'm right about who you are, this is not your first attempt to change your relationship with sugar. You have tried something. Maybe you've tried several somethings. Plans, boundaries, resets, streaks. Maybe they worked — some of them worked quite well. Maybe the best one is still working right now, in the sense that you are mostly keeping to it, mostly hitting the targets, mostly recovering quickly when you don't.

So why are you here?

That's not a trick question. It deserves a real answer, and only you can give it. But let me venture a guess: you are here because you are tired. Not tired of sugar. Tired of the *conversation* about sugar — the one that runs in your head before a dessert menu arrives, the one that resumes after a birthday party, the one that negotiates exceptions on holidays and then negotiates the return to normal on the Monday after. You're tired of anticipating, bargaining, and starting over.

If that phrase landed, sit with it for a moment. *Anticipating, bargaining, and starting over.* That is the cycle. Not bingeing and purging, necessarily. Not dramatic collapse. Just the quiet, grinding labor of managing a substance that seems to require permanent supervision, as though BAD SUGAR were a difficult employee you can never quite fire.

Here is what I want you to know before we go any further: this book is not a plan. It does not contain a meal schedule, a points system, a list of approved snacks, a detox calendar, or a phase. It will not ask you to go shopping for approved alternatives before Chapter 3. It will not ask you to begin anything at all — not yet, and not until you decide you're ready.

What it offers instead is an investigation. A genuine one. You are going to examine whether BAD SUGAR — the specific substance this book targets, which we'll define precisely in the next chapter — actually delivers the pleasure, comfort, and reward you believe it does. Not whether it's "bad for you." You already know plenty about that. The question is simpler and stranger: *does it do what you think it does?*

If it does, you'll know. And you'll put this book down and carry on exactly as before, having lost nothing.

If it doesn't — if the pleasure turns out to be less than it appears, or different from what you assumed — then something remarkable becomes possible. Not a tighter cage. Not a better set of rules. Something closer to the door swinging open.

But we aren't there yet. We aren't anywhere near there. Right now, I'm only asking you to look.

## TWO REPORTS

Let me share something that may sound familiar.

Nell Kauls is a woman who described her experience with food and sugar in a 2024 interview on the *Dishing Up Nutrition* podcast. She had done real work on her eating. She had established boundaries, built habits, learned what to avoid. And she had experienced genuine relief. In her own words: "I feel like I've been let out of jail."

That's a powerful metaphor. And I believe her. The obsessive food thoughts had loosened their grip. She wasn't living in the old torment anymore. That is a real and valuable change, and if you've experienced something similar — if your current system has brought you real peace compared to where you were — I have no interest in mocking it or calling it fraudulent. It worked. It is working.

But notice what she also described: a long maintenance history. Daily actions to keep cravings at bay. Firm personal boundaries she had to enforce. Special occasions that remained challenging. Exceptions that happened — and a practiced return to her usual plan at the next meal or the next day.

Does any of that sound like freedom to you? Or does it sound like a well-managed truce?

I ask that without judgment. A well-managed truce is vastly better than chaos. If your life with sugar has moved from open warfare to a stable ceasefire, you have achieved something most people never do. The boundaries are real. The relief is real.

And the labor is also real. The monitoring. The daily decisions. The awareness that holidays and celebrations still require a strategy. The quiet knowledge that if you stopped managing, the old pattern would likely return — because the thing you're managing against hasn't actually changed. It's still there, behind the fence you built, and the fence still needs mending.

Now consider a different report.

Ryan Andrews is a registered dietitian who wrote publicly about his own relationship with dessert. Before making a change, he estimated he spent at least two hours every week debating whether to eat dessert. Two hours. Not eating it — *debating* it. Running the negotiation. Weighing the exception against the rule. Two hours of a working professional's week, consumed by a single recurring question about food.

He eventually went twelve months without desserts, sweets, candy, or added sugar — across birthdays, major holidays, all of it. What struck me about his account was not the duration. It was a phrase he used about one month in. He described "not missing desserts."

Not *resisting* desserts. Not *managing* desserts. Not missing them.

Do you hear the difference? It's not a difference of behavior. Both Kauls and Andrews changed what they ate. The difference is in the experience of the change. One is maintained from the outside, through structure and effort and daily renewal. The other seems to have shifted on the inside — the dessert was simply removed from the active decision catalog, as though the question had been answered and didn't need to be asked anymore.

I want to be careful here. These are two people with two different targets, two different approaches, two different lives. I am not comparing methods, and I am certainly not promising you will reproduce either result. What I am pointing to is a distinction — a real and important one:

**There is a difference between not eating BAD SUGAR and not wanting it.**

There is a difference between the debate being managed and the debate being over.

## WHAT THIS BOOK IS ACTUALLY FOR

Most approaches to sugar operate on the same assumption: that you want the sugar, that wanting it is natural, and that the job is to build walls strong enough to keep you from acting on the wanting. Better walls, smarter walls, walls with cheat days built in so the pressure doesn't blow them apart. The want stays. The walls get more sophisticated.

This book asks a different question. What if the want itself is worth examining?

Not suppressing. Not overriding. *Examining.* The way you'd examine any belief you've held for a long time — by looking directly at it, without panic, and asking: is this actually true? Does BAD SUGAR deliver a special, irreplaceable pleasure? Does it rescue a bad day? Does it make a celebration complete? Or have I simply believed those things for so long that I've never tested them?

You can test whether BAD SUGAR owns any special or irreplaceable pleasure, without denying pleasant taste or giving up your right to choose.

Read that again. I mean it as a contract between us. You do not have to deny that sweet things taste pleasant — of course they do. You do not have to surrender your autonomy — you will choose, at every stage, what to do and when. And you do not have to take my word for anything. What you do have to do is look. Honestly. With curiosity rather than dread.

If you can do that, something becomes possible that no diet can offer: freedom without deprivation, without battle, and without a lifetime of rules.

That is the destination this book points toward. Not perfect control. Not ingredient-level purity. Not a medical protocol or a weight-loss prescription. Just this: a life in which BAD SUGAR is no longer an active proposition — no longer a reward to anticipate, a temptation to negotiate, a lapse to recover from. Not because you can't have it, but because you've seen through whatever made it seem essential.

That may sound outlandish. It may sound like the kind of promise that belongs on the cover of the last book that disappointed you. Good. Hold that skepticism. It will protect you. All I ask is that you carry it as curiosity rather than as a locked door — be skeptical *and* willing to look.

I should tell you plainly what this book does not carry. It does not carry clinical credentials. It does not carry a success-rate statistic. It does not carry a guarantee that your experience will match anyone else's. What it carries is an argument — one you can test for yourself, at your own pace, in your own kitchen, with nothing to lose.

## THE ROAD BETWEEN HERE AND THERE

You may be wondering what happens next. Will there be a meal plan by Chapter 4? A pantry purge by Chapter 6? A weigh-in?

No.

The next chapter will define the target precisely — what exactly falls under BAD SUGAR and, just as importantly, what doesn't. After that, we will begin examining the beliefs that give BAD SUGAR its hold: the pleasures it supposedly provides, the comforts it supposedly delivers, the social functions it supposedly serves. We will examine each one honestly, without rushing to a verdict and without asking you to stop eating anything while we look.

You are an investigator. Not a patient. Not a recruit. You are someone running an experiment on your own beliefs, with full authority to walk away at any point. That is the only stance this book respects, and it is the only stance from which real freedom — not the imposed kind, the discovered kind — can emerge.

## YOUR FIRST INSTRUCTION

There is only one instruction for now, and it may be the easiest you've ever been given by a book about sugar:

**1ST INSTRUCTION: READ WITH CURIOSITY, NOT DREAD; UNTIL YOU REACH YOUR CHOSEN THRESHOLD, EAT AS YOU NORMALLY DO, BEGIN NO NEW RESTRICTION OR COMPENSATION PLAN FOR THIS METHOD, AND KEEP FOLLOWING ANY QUALIFIED CLINICAL INSTRUCTION.**

Let that sink in. You are not starting a diet today. You are not cutting anything out today. If a doctor or dietitian has given you medical guidance, keep following it — that sits outside this investigation entirely. But as far as this book is concerned, you change nothing about your eating until a point much later, which you yourself will choose when you're ready.

Why? Because every plan you've tried before asked you to change the behavior first and sort out the wanting later. That's backwards. It puts the wall up before you've examined whether the thing behind it deserves to be fenced in. No wonder the walls keep needing repair.

We're going to do it the other way around. We examine the wanting first. If the examination does its job — if BAD SUGAR turns out to be less than you believed — the behavior change will follow naturally, easily, as the obvious thing to do. Not the hard thing. The obvious thing.

So eat your lunch. Have your usual afternoon snack. If you were going to have dessert tonight, have it. All I ask is that you keep reading with an open mind and notice, as you eat, whether the experience actually matches what you anticipated. Just notice.

That's all. For now, that is everything.
