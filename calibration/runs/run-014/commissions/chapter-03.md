# AUTHORITATIVE SEMANTIC COMMISSION — CH-03

CH-03, **Pleasant Is Not the Same as Necessary**, owns one evaluation-axis switch: the reader must stop treating pleasant taste as automatic proof that BAD SUGAR supplies an important, necessary, or irreplaceable life benefit. The chapter must resolve the inference “it tastes good, therefore removing it would deprive me of something important” without denying the taste, its calories, or the reader’s experience.

The distinction is firm: a sensation can be pleasant without being necessary, and liking a particular taste does not establish that BAD SUGAR uniquely owns pleasure, completes an occasion, or supplies something ordinary life cannot provide. The chapter need not prove that another food reproduces the exact sensory signature. Exact sensory difference is not the same as irreplaceable value, and no consolation product is required. Equally, the chapter may not pretend that a pleasant taste is imaginary or meaningless. Its claim is the non-equivalence of pleasantness and necessity, not the nonexistence of pleasantness.

The operational line established in CH-02 governs every use of “sugar”: the inquiry concerns BAD SUGAR, not whole fruit, ordinary meals, ordinary carbohydrates, incidental ingredients, or molecular purity. Medical glucose, prescribed nutrition, and qualified clinical or eating-disorder care remain outside the argument and always take priority. No consumption change, forced tasting, restriction exercise, or monitoring assignment belongs here. Moderation remains the reader’s right; this chapter tests a value claim rather than commanding a choice.

The primary recognition belongs to PER-01 and PER-04: the reader who anticipates dessert because it promises a special pleasure, and the informed reader who wants an evidence-honest inquiry rather than another dietary doctrine. Carry RD-01 exactly as frozen original reader voice, not as a participant quotation:

> “I check dessert before I have even chosen dinner.”

Debut M-04 exactly:

> `a special pleasure or rescue`

Here it names the disputed benefit claim. “Rescue” must not imply an established physiological mechanism, and the chapter must not yet branch into the later arguments about reward, comfort, celebration, or emotional relief.

Echo M-01 exactly, preserving its outcome-neutral permission:

> `You can test whether BAD SUGAR owns any special or irreplaceable pleasure, without denying pleasant taste or giving up your right to choose.`

Install I-03 verbatim:

> **QUESTION BOTH SIDES. Test whether BAD SUGAR owns a special or irreplaceable benefit; do not accept this book, a label, an advertisement, or a familiar rule blindly, and never disregard qualified clinical care.**

The required meta-objection is:

> “How do I know this book is not simply replacing one dogma with another?”

The answer must rest on separable, falsifiable claims, transparent evidence grades, and genuine permission to disagree—not on the book’s authority. A report that someone enjoyed or returned for a taste establishes the reported experience or attribution; it does not establish necessity. Evidence that suppressing sweet taste reduces reported pleasure counts against any denial of taste; it does not prove addiction or irreplaceability. A null whole-cohort imaging result limits what may be generalized from that trial; it does not prove that no individual or subgroup effect exists. Evidence specifically demonstrating a unique, important benefit owned by BAD SUGAR would matter to the inquiry and may not be defined away. The chapter must argue the value distinction confidently while preserving uncertainty about scientific mechanisms and the reader’s freedom to judge the larger claim.

EV-L01 is **NON-OUTCOME LIVED JUSTIFICATION**. It permits honest recognition of taste, anticipatory attention, and the credit individuals assign to taste. Its source material is:

- **S-004#E-001:** In Nelson I, Kruger R, Hsiao D, Stice E, and Ali A, *Nutrients* 17 (2025), 2718, seven self-selected adults completed 28 interviews in a small qualitative intervention. An anonymous participant said, exactly, at Table 1 under “pleasure-seeking motivation”: “There’s always room for dessert. If I go out for dinner, I have to look at the dessert menu before I order my mains.” This can establish one participant’s anticipatory attention, not a universal dessert need or causal loop.

- **S-009#E-001:** In Sarhan MM et al., *Frontiers in Nutrition* 11 (2024), 1388918, nineteen students aged 18–25 at one Saudi university were interviewed. Participant P2 said, exactly, in Results, “Taste” (§sec18): “I tried to cut down on soft drinks, but I start drinking them again for the taste.” This is a direct account of returning after a reduction attempt with taste given as the reason. The study did not test solutions or establish durable cessation outcomes.

- **S-020#E-005:** In Madigan CD et al., *Nutrients* 15(6), 1389, one participant, identified in the publication as Female, 61 years, described eating an extra slice after tennis and afternoon tea despite not being hungry, attributing it to taste. This is an interpretation grounded at Results §3.3, “Excess Food.” The exact permitted fragment is “for the taste of it”; surrounding language must remain paraphrase rather than a reconstructed quotation. The study concerned indulgent foods and beverages broadly, and this account does not establish physiological hunger, causation, or a general pattern.

These accounts may make the reader feel accurately seen, but they cannot establish clinical compulsion, addiction, an ordinary craving mechanism, universal anticipation, or any quitting result. In particular, anticipation may be shown as an observed fact, but its causal explanation and the later open-loop mechanism do not belong here.

EV-S04 is **MIXED**. Its permitted conclusion is that sweet taste can affect reported pleasure. Pleasure findings are not addiction findings:

- In **S-004**, blocking sweet taste often reduced reported pleasure in the small qualitative intervention. This supports admitting a real sensory contribution. It does not show that the pleasure is irreplaceable, addictive, or evidence that this book’s method works.

- **S-005#E-001**, Yunker AG, Alves JM, Luo S, et al., *JAMA Network Open* 4 (2021), e2126313, was a randomized crossover human fMRI trial in which seventy-four adults received sucrose, sweetness-matched sucralose, and water. The whole-cohort neural result states exactly, at “Neural BOLD results, whole cohort”: “We did not observe significant differences in BOLD signal to any food cue contrasts”. Prespecified BMI and sex interactions were reported, so the whole-cohort result must not be converted into a universal null claim. The trial does not show that sucrose causes craving or addiction, nor does it establish the opposite.

The scientific material should demonstrate epistemic discipline, not furnish a brain-based argument. Do not invoke dopamine, hijacking, withdrawal, biochemical crashes, settled addiction, or any other mechanism absent from the accepted evidence. Do not use a lack of general imaging difference as proof that taste is unreal, or the pleasure reports as proof that BAD SUGAR is necessary.

AN-12, **one bright note claiming the whole song**, is the assigned image. Its job is to preserve the bright note while denying its automatic claim over the whole experience. It may distinguish sensory contribution from ownership of a meal or occasion, but it must not become the full pleasure-source audit or the book’s strongest restaurant-dessert confrontation. Those broader reassignments remain later work.

The chapter’s authority posture must remain inquiry-based. It has no author escape story, clinical pedigree, success rate, or guaranteed outcome to invoke. First-person guide language may promise honesty or frame the test, but it may not invent testimony. Permission to disagree must be substantive: the writer may not offer nominal choice while portraying dissent as weakness, irrationality, or proof of addiction.

Keep the freedom curve low. This chapter does not announce that the debate is over, ask for a threshold choice, or promise that occasions will feel easy. It receives a stable operational definition of BAD SUGAR and hands forward one clean understanding: pleasant sensation is real, while special ownership, necessity, and irreplaceability are separate claims that pleasantness alone cannot prove.
